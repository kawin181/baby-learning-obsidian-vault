# Pacifier Use & Safety in Newborns — NHS

## Key Points
- NHS recommends thinking about using a dummy at sleep time — some research suggests it may lower SIDS risk (NHS uses more cautious wording than AAP)
- Introduce only after breastfeeding is established (a few weeks)
- Offer at every sleep — day and night — for consistent benefit
- Stop using dummy by 6–12 months (NHS main guidance) / 10–12 months (NHS Lothian)
- Never put anything sweet on the dummy — risk of tooth decay
- Never use a neck cord
- Extended dummy use may contribute to dental problems and speech sound difficulties
- Babies using dummies have fewer opportunities to babble — reduce use when baby is communicating

## Raw Content

### SIDS Risk
NHS states: "think about using a dummy to settle your baby to sleep – some research suggests this can help lower the risk of SIDS." Note the more cautious language compared to AAP — the NHS acknowledges the evidence is "not strong" and "not all experts agree."

### Timing — Breastfeeding Interaction
Only introduce a dummy once breastfeeding is established, which may take a few weeks. Once breastfeeding is established, introducing a dummy should not negatively affect it.

### Regular Use Recommendation
"Regular dummy use" = offering at every sleep time, both day and night. Consistent use appears to be the most protective approach.

### When to Stop
- NHS (main): stop by 6–12 months
- NHS Lothian guidance: "Try to stop using a dummy by the time your baby is 10–12 months"
- Earlier cessation is easier; weaning becomes progressively harder with age

### Safety Rules
- Do not put sweet substances on dummy — promotes tooth decay
- Do not use neck cord — strangulation hazard
- Do not use clips attached to clothing or objects

### Developmental Concerns
- Babies using dummies have reduced babbling time
- Extended use may contribute to dental misalignment and speech sound development delays
- Practical advice: remove dummy when baby is actively babbling or communicating; restrict to sleep times as baby grows

### Weaning Strategies (NHS Lothian)
- Gradual reduction
- Restrict to bedtime / illness
- Positive reinforcement (sticker charts)
- Creative exchanges for older toddlers
