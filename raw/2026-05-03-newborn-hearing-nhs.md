# Newborn Hearing Development & Screening — NHS

## Key Points
- 1–2 babies per 1,000 births have permanent hearing loss in UK
- Rate rises to ~1 in 100 for babies requiring intensive care (NICU) beyond 48 hours
- NHS screens all newborns — ideally within first few weeks (4–5 weeks), or before hospital discharge
- Tests: AOAE (quick, few minutes) and AABR (5–15 minutes, more detailed)
- Most babies with hearing loss have no family history
- Unclear result at first test does NOT mean hearing loss — may be fluid in ear, movement, noise
- Referral to audiology within 4 weeks if no clear response from either/both ears
- Not all permanent hearing loss is detected at birth — some can develop later

## Raw Content

### Prevalence
- 1–2 per 1,000 births: permanent hearing loss
- 1 in 100: babies who required NICU stay > 48 hours
- Most cases have no identifiable family history

### Timing
- Ideally between 4–5 weeks of age
- If born in hospital: may be tested before discharge
- Can be done up to 3 months of age

### Test Types

**AOAE (Automated Otoacoustic Emission):**
- Soft earpiece in baby's ear
- Gentle clicking sounds played
- Measures echo response from inner ear hair cells
- Takes just a few minutes
- Suitable even if infant is awake
- Can give unclear result if baby is restless or there is background noise

**AABR (Automated Auditory Brainstem Response):**
- Three sensors placed on head and neck
- Soft headphones play clicking sounds
- Measures how auditory nerve and brain respond
- Takes 5–15 minutes
- Baby should be settled or asleep

### If Result is Unclear or Referral Needed
- First unclear result: referred for repeat AOAE or AABR
- If still no clear response: audiology appointment within 4 weeks
- Specialist evaluation: 1–2 hours; does not mean definite hearing loss
- Many babies who don't pass initial screen have normal hearing at follow-up (fluid in ear, movement artefacts)

### Screening Limitations
The NHS newborn hearing screen does not detect all types of permanent hearing loss. Some hearing loss is progressive or late-onset. Parents should contact GP if they notice:
- Baby does not startle to loud noises
- Baby is not calmed by voice by 2–3 months
- Baby does not turn toward sounds by 4 months
