# Diaper Rash — NHS (UK)

## Key Points
- Nappy rash presents as red or raw patches on baby's bottom or whole nappy area
- Skin appears sore, warm to touch; may be scaly or dry; causes visible discomfort
- Signs may be less visible on darker skin tones
- Causes: prolonged urine/feces contact, infrequent changes, friction, reactions to products, medications (antibiotics), fungal/bacterial infection
- Prevention: prompt changes, keep area clean/dry, allow nappy-free time, highly absorbent nappies, cleanse with water or alcohol-free/fragrance-free wipes
- Avoid: soaps, lotions, bubble bath, talcum powder, antiseptics, overly tight nappies
- Pharmacist can recommend barrier creams; paracetamol for pain only in babies over 2 months
- GP may prescribe: steroid cream, antifungal, or antibiotics depending on type
- Seek care if rash worsens, spreads, persists, accompanied by fever, or causes significant distress

## Raw Content
### Symptoms
- Red or raw patches on bottom or whole nappy area
- Sore and warm feeling skin
- Scaly or dry patches
- Baby appears uncomfortable, especially during diaper changes

### Causes
1. Prolonged contact with urine or faeces
2. Infrequent nappy changes
3. Inadequate cleaning
4. Friction from nappy
5. Reactions to soaps, detergents, fragranced products
6. Certain medications (antibiotics, laxatives)
7. Fungal (candida) or bacterial infections

### Prevention (NHS Guidelines)
- Change wet/soiled nappies promptly
- Keep area clean and dry by gentle patting — do not rub
- Allow nappy-free time when feasible (lay baby on clean towel)
- Use highly absorbent, well-fitted nappies
- Cleanse with water or fragrance-free, alcohol-free wipes
- Daily bath (not more than twice daily)

### What to Avoid
- Soaps, lotions, bubble bath — irritate sensitive skin
- Talcum powder — inhalation risk and skin irritation
- Antiseptics — can damage delicate skin
- Overly tight nappies — restricts airflow

### Treatment
- Barrier cream: pharmacist can recommend appropriate products
- If yeast infection suspected: antifungal (e.g., miconazole) from GP
- If bacterial: antibiotics from GP
- Mild steroid cream only on GP prescription

### When to See a GP
- Rash worsens despite 48–72 hours of home care
- Rash spreads beyond diaper area
- Fever accompanying the rash
- Significant pain or distress
- Pus, open sores, or blistering
