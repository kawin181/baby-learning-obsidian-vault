# Umbilical Cord Care

## Summary
After birth, the umbilical cord stump dries and falls off naturally within 1–3 weeks. In hospital-born infants in high-resource settings, the AAP and WHO recommend dry cord care — keeping the stump clean, dry, and exposed to air without applying any antiseptics. Knowing the warning signs of infection (omphalitis) is critical for new parents.

## What Research Says
The current evidence-based standard for cord care in developed countries is **dry cord care** — no alcohol, no antiseptics, no lotions. Studies in high-resource hospital settings show dry care is as safe or safer than antiseptic application (AAP, 2016 policy statement in *Pediatrics*).

In low-resource community settings with high neonatal mortality (e.g., parts of Asia and Africa), WHO recommends applying 4% chlorhexidine to the stump within 24 hours of birth, repeated daily for 3 days. This intervention significantly reduces omphalitis rates and neonatal mortality (WHO, 2014).

The cord typically progresses through color changes — yellowish-green → brown → black — as it desiccates. A tiny amount of dried blood at the base is normal. Average separation time is 7–21 days.

## Practical Guidelines
1. **Keep it dry** — Expose the stump to air as much as possible. Avoid covering it with clothing.
2. **Fold the diaper down** — Use newborn diapers with a cord notch cut-out, or fold the front of the diaper below the stump line.
3. **Sponge baths only** — No tub baths until the cord falls off completely.
4. **Do not pull** — Even when the cord appears nearly detached, let it separate on its own.
5. **Do not apply alcohol** — No antiseptics, talcum powder, or traditional herbal applications.
6. **Clean if soiled** — If the area gets dirty, wipe gently with a damp cloth and pat dry immediately.

## Thai Context
Traditional practices in some Thai households include applying herbal preparations (สมุนไพร) or powder to the cord stump. Current Thai Ministry of Public Health (กรมอนามัย) guidance aligns with WHO recommendations: dry cord care for hospital-born infants. Families should be counseled to avoid herbal applications, which can introduce pathogens and cause omphalitis.

## Warning Signs 🚨
Seek immediate medical attention if any of these appear:
- Redness spreading more than 5 mm from the base of the stump
- Pus or foul-smelling yellow/green discharge
- Swelling or warmth around the belly button
- Fever: rectal temperature ≥ 38°C (100.4°F)
- Baby is lethargic, not feeding, or appears ill
