# Diaper Rash — AAP / HealthyChildren.org

## Key Points
- At least 50% of all babies experience diaper rash — most common reason parents seek pediatric care for skin issues
- 5 main types: irritant dermatitis, yeast (candida), bacterial (impetigo), allergic reaction, rare systemic conditions
- Irritant dermatitis is by far the most common — caused by prolonged contact with urine and stool
- Yeast infection features shiny bright-red patches with sharp borders that worsen in groin folds; often follows antibiotic use
- Bacterial rash (strep or staph) needs medical confirmation and targeted treatment
- Prevention: frequent diaper changes, gentle cleansing (no alcohol/fragrance), thick zinc oxide barrier paste
- Treatment: apply zinc oxide barrier paste thickly "like icing on a cupcake" — do not wipe off clean paste at each change
- Do NOT use over-the-counter antibiotic ointments without doctor guidance (can worsen condition)
- Contact doctor if rash persists or worsens after 2–3 days, or if there are signs of infection

## Raw Content
### Types in Detail

**1. Irritant Dermatitis (most common)**
- Pink/red patches in diaper area; groin folds usually spared
- Worse with diarrhea or teething phases

**2. Yeast / Candida**
- Shiny, bright-red patches with sharp edges
- Affects groin folds (key differentiator from irritant rash)
- May have small satellite bumps or oozing sores
- Risk: after antibiotic therapy

**3. Bacterial Impetigo**
- Strep → bright red ring around anus
- Staph → yellow crusting, pimple-like lesions
- Requires prescription treatment

**4. Allergic Reaction**
- Pattern matches where product contacts skin
- Common triggers: dyes, elastics, fragrances, preservatives
- Try switching diaper brand for 2 weeks as diagnostic test

**5. Rare**
- Seborrheic dermatitis, psoriasis, zinc-deficiency conditions

### Prevention
1. Change diapers frequently — immediately after bowel movements
2. Clean with alcohol-free, fragrance-free wipes or plain warm water
3. Pat dry gently — do not rub
4. Apply thick barrier paste (zinc oxide or petrolatum) at every change
5. Do not over-tighten diaper — allow airflow
6. Bathe daily if area is irritated

### Treatment
- Thick zinc oxide paste: apply heavily, like icing on a cake
- Do not scrub off paste at next change if not soiled — clean carefully over it
- Continue until rash resolves
- Antifungal cream (clotrimazole or miconazole) if yeast confirmed
- Mild hydrocortisone 0.5–1% for 3–5 days if prescribed

### When to Call Pediatrician
- Rash not improving after 2–3 days of home treatment
- Presence of blisters, pimples, pus, crusting, or open sores
- Bright rash developing during antibiotic course
- Baby appears very uncomfortable or has fever
