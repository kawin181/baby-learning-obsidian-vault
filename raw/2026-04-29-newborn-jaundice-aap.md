# Newborn Jaundice — AAP (2022 Revised Clinical Practice Guideline)

## Key Points
- **>80% of newborns** develop some degree of jaundice — it is usually physiologic (normal)
- Caused by elevated **bilirubin** (breakdown product of fetal hemoglobin)
- Physiologic jaundice appears day **2–3**, peaks day **3–5**, resolves by **1–2 weeks** in term infants
- **Breast milk jaundice** can persist up to 3 months — distinct from pathologic jaundice; do not stop breastfeeding
- Treatment for significant hyperbilirubinemia: **phototherapy** (blue-green light breaks down bilirubin in skin)
- 2022 AAP guideline **raised thresholds** for phototherapy initiation — bilirubin must reach higher levels than 2004 before treatment recommended
- **G6PD deficiency** now recognized as a major risk factor — especially relevant in Southeast Asian, African, and Middle Eastern populations
- **Isoimmune hemolytic disease** (blood type incompatibility — ABO or Rh) is an important cause of severe jaundice
- Risk stratified by gestational age, age in hours, and neurotoxicity risk factors
- **Exchange transfusion** reserved for severe cases not responding to intensive phototherapy

## Raw Content
### Bilirubin Physiology
At birth, fetal hemoglobin (HbF) is broken down and replaced by adult hemoglobin. This produces large amounts of unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin, which the immature neonatal liver cannot clear efficiently. Unconjugated bilirubin deposits in skin → yellow discoloration.

### Risk Stratification (2022 AAP)
All newborns ≥35 weeks should have bilirubin measured at ≥24 hours of age. Risk is stratified by:
1. Total serum bilirubin (TSB) or transcutaneous bilirubin (TcB) level
2. Age in hours (not days)
3. Neurotoxicity risk factors: isoimmune hemolytic disease, G6PD deficiency, asphyxia, sepsis, hypoalbuminemia

### G6PD Deficiency — Thai Context
G6PD deficiency is X-linked and **prevalent in Southeast Asia including Thailand** — incidence up to 10–15% in some Thai populations. Babies with G6PD deficiency can develop rapid, severe hyperbilirubinemia with minimal warning. All Thai newborns should be screened; screen result may not be available before discharge.

### Phototherapy Mechanism
Blue-green light (peak absorption 450–490 nm) converts bilirubin in the skin into water-soluble isomers that can be excreted without liver conjugation. Intensive phototherapy uses high-irradiance lamps on as much skin surface as possible.

### Escalation of Care Threshold
When TSB reaches a higher threshold (defined by AAP nomogram), hydration support + intensive phototherapy is indicated. IVIG may be used in isoimmune hemolytic disease.
