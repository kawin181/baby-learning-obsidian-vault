# Newborn Weight Gain & Growth Monitoring

## Summary
Newborns normally lose up to 10% of their birth weight in the first week due to fluid loss, then regain it by 2 weeks of age. After that, healthy infants gain approximately 150–210 g (5–7 oz) per week for the first 3 months. Clinicians use WHO growth charts and track growth trajectory — not a single target weight — to assess a baby's health.

## What Research Says
**Initial weight loss:**
- Weight loss of up to 10% of birth weight in the first 5–7 days is normal (AAP/Medical News Today)
- Breastfed babies may lose slightly more than formula-fed babies
- 50% of newborns are back to birth weight by days 9–10; most by day 14 (AAP, Flaherman et al. 2016)
- 14% of vaginally delivered and 24% of cesarean-delivered infants are not back to birth weight by day 14

**Expected growth rate (0–3 months):**
- Average weight gain: ~150–210 g/week (~5–7 oz/week, approximately 1 oz/day)
- By 1 month: females ~4.2 kg / males ~4.5 kg
- By 3 months: females ~5.8 kg / males ~6.4 kg
- Most babies double their birth weight by 3–4 months

**Growth monitoring tools:**
- AAP and CDC recommend WHO Multicentre Growth Reference Study charts for all term infants 0–24 months
- WHO charts represent optimal growth under ideal conditions (breastfed, non-smoking, healthy environment)
- Z scores are the most statistically accurate way to assess nutritional status
- Flag growth that falls below the 2.3rd or above the 97.7th percentile for clinical review

## Practical Guidelines
- **Weigh at birth, then at 3–5 days, 2 weeks, and every 1–2 months** — follow your clinic's schedule
- At each visit, plot weight on a WHO growth chart and check the trajectory
- Accept normal initial weight loss of up to 10%; worry if it exceeds 10% or doesn't recover by day 14
- Focus on the growth curve trend, not a specific number on the scale
- Signs of adequate nutrition: 6+ wet diapers/day by day 5–6, regular bowel movements, alert and active when awake
- If concerned, weigh before and after a breastfeeding session to assess milk transfer

## Thai Context
- กรมอนามัย recommends using the WHO growth reference charts at all well-child visits (สมุดบันทึกสุขภาพแม่และเด็ก — the pink book)
- Thai pediatric growth charts in the pink maternal-child health book are based on WHO standards
- Normal Thai newborn birth weight range: 2.5–4.0 kg; babies <2.5 kg are classified as low birth weight and need closer monitoring
- Thai hospitals typically conduct weight checks at 1 month, 2 months, and 4 months as part of the EPI immunization schedule

## Warning Signs 🚨
- Weight loss >10% of birth weight
- Not back to birth weight by 14 days
- Fewer than 6 wet diapers per day after day 5
- Baby seems persistently hungry, lethargic, or jaundiced
- Weight drops across 2 or more major percentile lines on the growth chart
- No weight gain at all for 2 consecutive weeks
- Parents or caregivers are concerned
