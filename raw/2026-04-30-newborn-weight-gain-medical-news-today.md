# Newborn Weight Gain & Growth Monitoring — Medical News Today

## Key Points
- Average birth weight: 7 lb 6 oz (3.3 kg) for males, 7 lb 2 oz (3.2 kg) for females
- Normal to lose ~10% of birth weight in the first week — mainly due to fluid loss
- Most infants regain birth weight by approximately 2 weeks of age
- Rapid weight gain occurs during the first 6 months of life
- By 1 month: females ~9 lb 4 oz (4.2 kg); males ~9 lb 14 oz (4.5 kg)
- By 3 months: females ~12 lb 14 oz (5.8 kg); males ~14 lb 1 oz (6.4 kg)
- Healthcare providers look for steady growth trajectory — not a specific target percentile
- Weight is assessed alongside length, head circumference, and developmental milestones
- Medical conditions affecting nutrient absorption can impair growth

## Raw Content
Newborns are expected to lose weight in the first few days of life. A weight loss of around 10% is considered normal. This is primarily due to fluid loss after birth and is not a cause for concern in healthy newborns.

Babies generally regain their birth weight within the first 1–2 weeks. After that, healthy infants gain weight most rapidly during the first 6 months. Healthcare providers track weight using growth charts and assess whether a baby's growth follows a consistent percentile curve over time.

**Typical weight milestones:**
- Birth: 5 lb 8 oz – 8 lb 13 oz (2.5–4.0 kg) for full-term babies
- 1 month: ~9 lb 4 oz females / ~9 lb 14 oz males
- 3 months: ~12 lb 14 oz females / ~14 lb 1 oz males

Doctors do not focus on hitting a specific weight number but rather on consistent upward growth trajectory. If a baby drops more than 2 percentile lines on the growth chart, further evaluation is warranted.
