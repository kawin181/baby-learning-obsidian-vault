# Newborn Skin Care & Baby Bath

## Summary
Newborns do not need a full bath every day — three times a week is sufficient, and daily "top and tail" washing covers the rest. The first bath should be delayed at least 24 hours after birth to protect vernix and support breastfeeding. Safety during bathing is paramount: never leave a baby alone in water, and avoid bath seats.

## What Research Says
- **First bath delay (WHO / AAP):** Wait at least 24 hours after birth; minimum 6 hours if cultural practices require earlier bathing. Vernix (white coating on newborn skin) is a natural antimicrobial moisturizer — leaving it on for 24 hours reduces skin dryness and infection risk. A 12-hour delay has been associated with a 166% increase in breastfeeding success (AAP HealthyChildren)
- **Frequency:** 3 baths/week is sufficient for infants; bathing more often strips natural oils and causes dry skin (AAP)
- **Sponge bath only** until umbilical cord stump falls off (typically 1–2 weeks)
- **Drowning risk:** Babies can drown in only a few centimeters of water; most bathtub drownings involve children under 1 year (NHS / RoSPA)
- **Products:** Neutral-pH, fragrance-free, baby-formulated products only; adult soaps and bubble baths disrupt the skin barrier
- **Bath seats** are not recommended by NHS RoSPA — they can tip and do not provide safety

## Practical Guidelines

### Daily Hygiene — Top and Tail (ทำทุกวัน)
1. Keep baby wrapped; expose only the area being washed
2. **Eyes:** Damp cotton wool, one piece per eye; wipe from inner corner outward (nose → ear direction)
3. **Ears:** Clean around outer ear only with cotton wool; never insert anything inside
4. **Face and neck:** Warm water, no soap; pay attention to neck skin folds
5. **Hands:** Warm water and cotton wool
6. **Genitals/bottom:** Warm water and cotton wool; girls — always wipe front to back
7. **Umbilical cord:** Clean only if soiled with urine or stool; warm water + cotton wool; pat completely dry

### Full Bath — Sponge Bath (until cord falls off)
1. Gather all supplies before starting; keep everything within arm's reach
2. Baby on flat padded surface
3. Wash face first with damp, soap-free cloth
4. Work top to bottom; keep unwashed areas wrapped in towel
5. Save diaper area for last

### Full Bath — Tub Bath (after cord heals)
1. Fill tub with 2–3 inches of warm water
2. Test temperature with wrist or elbow (not palm)
3. Set hot water heater ≤ 120°F (49°C) to prevent scalds
4. Turn off faucet before placing baby
5. Support head with one hand; lower baby feet-first
6. Wash quickly and gently; lift out and wrap immediately
7. Apply fragrance-free moisturizer while skin is still slightly damp

### Skin Care Products
- **Preferred:** Plain warm water for newborns (first 4–6 weeks)
- **If product needed:** Mild, fragrance-free, neutral-pH baby cleanser
- **Post-bath:** Fragrance-free hypoallergenic baby lotion (helps retain moisture)
- **Avoid:** Adult soaps, bubble baths, products with dyes/fragrances/alcohol

### Common Newborn Skin Conditions
| Condition | What it looks like | Action |
|-----------|-------------------|--------|
| **Vernix** | White creamy coating at birth | Leave on; wipes off naturally by day 2–3 |
| **Baby acne** | Small red/white pimples on face (weeks 2–4) | No treatment; gently wash; resolves within weeks |
| **Milia** | Tiny white dots on nose/cheeks | No treatment; resolves spontaneously |
| **Cradle cap** | Yellow-brown scaly patches on scalp | Harmless; soft-bristle brush to loosen; resolves months 6–12 |
| **Dry/peeling skin** | Skin peeling in first 1–2 weeks | Normal; no treatment; moisturizer helps |
| **Diaper rash** | Red irritated skin in diaper area | Keep dry; barrier cream (zinc oxide); see doctor if persists |

### Nail Care
- Use baby nail clippers or safety scissors (never adult nail clippers)
- Best done while baby sleeps — hands remain still
- File rather than cut in first 2 weeks if nails are very soft

## Warning Signs 🚨
- Umbilical cord area: redness, swelling, foul odor, or discharge — possible omphalitis (infection), see doctor
- Spreading rash with fever
- Yellow-crusted sores or blisters — possible impetigo (bacterial skin infection)
- Severe diaper rash not improving after 3 days of barrier cream
- Cradle cap that spreads beyond scalp to face/body
- Any burn from bath water (redness, blistering)
