# Baby Gas & Burping Techniques — Mayo Clinic

## Key Points
- Burp baby more frequently — during AND after feeding
- Feed in upright position; pause regularly for burping
- Curved bottles that collapse as they empty reduce air intake significantly
- Gas drops (simethicone): generally considered safe at 20 mg up to 4x/day
- Simethicone works by breaking up gas bubbles in stomach and intestines
- Experiment with different bottles and nipples — every baby is different
- Gassiness alone is rarely a sign of a serious problem

## Raw Content
### Burping During Feeding
- Burp bottle-fed babies every 2–3 oz
- Burp breastfed babies every time you switch breasts
- Hold baby upright with chin resting on your shoulder; rub or pat back gently
- Babies don't always burp — that's OK; some swallow less air than others

### Feeding Position
- Hold baby at roughly 45° angle during bottle feeding — keeps air at bottom of bottle
- Avoid feeding while baby is lying flat — more air is swallowed
- For breastfed babies: position that keeps baby's head higher than stomach helps drainage

### Bottle Selection
- Curved bottles that cave in as milk depletes help reduce air intake
- Vented bottles with internal straw-like system also effective
- Slow-flow nipples recommended for newborns to control intake pace

### Simethicone (Gas Drops)
- Active ingredient: simethicone — antifoaming agent that breaks up gas bubbles
- Mayo Clinic considers them generally safe for infant use
- Typical dose: 20 mg, up to 4 times per day
- Safe for daily use if needed
- Note: effectiveness vs. placebo is debated; some trials show minimal difference
- "If they seem to help your baby, there's no harm in using them" — Mayo Clinic guidance

### When to Call a Doctor
- Gas accompanied by vomiting, poor feeding, or failure to gain weight
- Blood in stool
- Inconsolable crying beyond 3 hours (may indicate colic or other condition)
