# Diaper Rash

## Summary
Diaper rash affects at least half of all babies and is the most common skin problem in infancy. The vast majority of cases are irritant dermatitis — caused by prolonged skin contact with urine and stool — and resolve within 2–3 days with frequent diaper changes, gentle cleansing, and a thick zinc oxide barrier paste. Yeast and bacterial rashes require specific treatments and should be evaluated by a doctor.

## What Research Says
- **Prevalence**: At least 50% of babies experience diaper rash (AAP).
- **Most common type**: Irritant contact dermatitis — red/pink patches in the diaper area with groin folds typically spared.
- **Yeast (Candida) infection**: Shiny, bright-red rash with sharp borders that affects groin folds; often follows antibiotic use; requires antifungal treatment.
- **Bacterial (Impetigo)**: Strep causes bright-red ring around anus; staph causes yellow-crusted lesions; requires prescription antibiotics.
- **Allergic reaction**: Matches pattern of product contact; test by switching diaper or wipe brand.
- **NHS guideline**: Avoid talcum powder (inhalation risk), antiseptics, and fragranced products in the diaper area.
- **Barrier cream**: Zinc oxide paste applied thickly at every change is the most evidence-supported prevention and treatment measure.

## Practical Guidelines
1. **Change often**: Replace wet or soiled diapers promptly — at minimum every 2–3 hours, immediately after every bowel movement.
2. **Cleanse gently**: Use warm water or alcohol-free, fragrance-free wipes. Pat dry — never rub.
3. **Apply zinc oxide**: Use thick barrier paste (zinc oxide or petrolatum) at every diaper change, applied like "icing on a cupcake."
4. **Don't remove clean paste**: If paste is not soiled at the next change, apply a new layer over the old — do not scrub off intact barrier.
5. **Allow air time**: Give at least 5–10 minutes of nappy-free time each day — lay baby on a clean towel.
6. **Correct diaper fit**: Not too tight — airflow inside the diaper helps prevent moisture buildup.
7. **Avoid irritants**: No soaps, bubble bath, talcum powder, antiseptics, or fragranced products in diaper area.

## Thai Context
- Thai mothers commonly apply traditional herbal pastes (ยาสมุนไพร) to diaper rash — this is not recommended; some can irritate or sensitize newborn skin.
- Zinc oxide cream (ครีมซิงค์ออกไซด์) is widely available at Thai pharmacies and is the recommended first-line barrier product.
- In Thailand, consult a pharmacist first; if rash persists > 3 days or shows signs of infection, visit a pediatrician at the nearest hospital.
- Thai climate (heat + humidity) increases risk of diaper rash — more frequent changes are especially important.

## Warning Signs 🚨
- Rash persists or worsens after 2–3 days of proper home treatment
- Blisters, pimples, pus, yellow crusting, or open sores
- Bright-red rash developing during antibiotic treatment (possible yeast)
- Rash spreading beyond the diaper area
- Baby has fever alongside the rash
- Baby appears very uncomfortable, especially during urination or diaper changes
- Rash in a baby under 6 weeks old → see doctor promptly
