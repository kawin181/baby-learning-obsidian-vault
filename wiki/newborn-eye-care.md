# Newborn Eye Care & Blocked Tear Ducts

## Summary
Blocked tear ducts (nasolacrimal duct obstruction) affect about 10% of newborns, causing watery and sticky eyes — but over 90% resolve on their own by 12 months without treatment. The main job of new parents is to distinguish normal discharge from infection, keep the area clean, and know when to call the doctor.

## What Research Says
The nasolacrimal duct drains tears from the eye into the nasal cavity. In newborns, a thin membrane at the duct's lower end sometimes fails to open at birth, causing tears to pool and stagnate. AAP notes that 30% of affected babies have bilateral blockage. Despite appearing worrying, the outcome is excellent: AAP reports >90% spontaneous resolution by 12 months; NHS data extend this to state that even unresolved cases at age 1 often open by themselves before age 5.

**Massage:** AAP states that lacrimal massage is not required — the duct will open without it. However, some pediatricians recommend gentle massage to help. If advised to massage, NHS Hull provides the evidence-based technique: small finger, downward stroke from the inner corner of the eye toward the mouth, up to 10 strokes twice daily.

**Infection:** Stagnant tears are a medium for bacterial growth. Secondary conjunctivitis is the main complication. Antibiotic eye drops (prescribed by GP) clear infection quickly, but do not open the duct itself.

## Practical Guidelines
1. **Hygiene first:** Wipe crusty discharge each morning with cooled boiled water on fresh cotton wool — always wipe from inner to outer corner
2. **One wipe per swipe:** Use a fresh piece of cotton wool for each stroke to avoid spreading bacteria
3. **Massage (if recommended by your pediatrician):** Wash hands → small finger → gentle downward press at inner eyelid corner → toward mouth → 10 strokes → twice daily
4. **Moisturise the skin** below the eye if it becomes red from constant tear spillage
5. **Watch for infection signs:** Yellow/green pus, red eyelid, lids stuck together → call your doctor for antibiotic drops
6. **12-month check:** If still blocked at 1 year, ask for ophthalmologist referral (probing procedure is simple, done in one day)

## Thai Context
Blocked tear ducts are often mistaken by Thai grandparents for "dirty eyes" or eye infections requiring traditional remedies. Breast milk is sometimes used as an eye drop in Thai folk medicine — this is **not recommended** by AAP or NHS. Breast milk does not open the duct and may introduce bacteria. Plain cooled boiled water is the appropriate cleaning agent.

## Warning Signs 🚨
- Yellow-green pus from the eye (not just clear stickiness) → GP same day for antibiotic drops
- Red, swollen eyelid that is hot to touch → urgent GP visit (risk of periorbital cellulitis)
- Baby rubbing the eye repeatedly and appearing in pain
- Discharge in both eyes from the first day of life → may indicate congenital eye infection, not simple blockage → same-day assessment
- Blocked duct still present at 12 months → ophthalmologist referral
