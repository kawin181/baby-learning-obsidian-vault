# Newborn Hearing Development & Screening

## Summary
Approximately 1–2 per 1,000 newborns are born with permanent hearing loss, making it one of the most common congenital conditions. All newborns should be screened before 1 month of age using OAE or ABR tests; if a concern is identified, full audiological evaluation should occur by 3 months. Early intervention by 6 months produces the best language development outcomes.

## What Research Says

### Prevalence and Importance
- NHS: 1–2 per 1,000 births have permanent hearing loss; rises to ~1 in 100 for babies needing NICU care beyond 48 hours
- AAP: ~6,000 US newborns per year are born with hearing impairment
- Most babies with hearing loss have no family history — universal screening is the only reliable detection method
- Hearing loss undetected past 6 months causes significant delays in speech, language, and cognitive development

### EHDI Benchmarks (AAP / CDC)
The "1-3-6" framework:
1. **Screen by 1 month** — ideally before hospital discharge (1–2 days of life)
2. **Diagnose by 3 months** — full audiological evaluation if screen failed or unclear
3. **Intervene by 6 months** — hearing aids, cochlear implant candidacy, speech-language therapy

Evidence: language outcomes at school age are significantly better when intervention begins before 6 months compared to later identification.

### Screening Tests

| Test | What It Measures | Duration | Baby State |
|------|-----------------|----------|------------|
| OAE / AOAE | Inner ear hair cell response (echo) | 2–5 min | Awake or asleep |
| ABR / AABR | Auditory nerve + brainstem response | 5–15 min | Asleep/settled |

**Unclear first result ≠ hearing loss.** Common causes of unclear results: fluid in the ear canal, baby movement, background noise. NHS data shows many babies who don't pass initial screen have normal hearing at follow-up.

### Risk Factors for Congenital Hearing Loss
- NICU stay > 48 hours (1 in 100 risk — 10× general population)
- Family history of childhood hearing loss
- Congenital infections (CMV, rubella, herpes, syphilis, toxoplasmosis)
- Craniofacial abnormalities (cleft palate, ear canal malformation)
- Hyperbilirubinemia requiring exchange transfusion
- Maternal ototoxic drug exposure during pregnancy

## Practical Guidelines

1. **Attend the newborn hearing screen** — in Thailand it is offered at many hospitals; do not skip or delay
2. **Understand "refer" ≠ deaf** — a failed first screen means more testing, not a diagnosis; most referred babies have normal hearing
3. **Act on referrals promptly** — audiology appointment should happen within 4 weeks of referral
4. **Watch developmental milestones between checkups:**
   - Newborn: startle (Moro reflex) to loud sounds
   - 1–2 months: calms to parent's voice; recognizes mother's voice
   - 3 months: turns eyes or head toward a sound source
5. **Never assume the startle reflex means full hearing** — a baby can startle at loud sounds yet still not hear frequencies needed for speech and language
6. **Report concerns anytime** — hearing loss can be progressive; if something feels off, ask for re-evaluation regardless of birth screen result

## Thai Context
Newborn hearing screening is part of the standard newborn care package in Thai government hospitals. However, uptake and follow-up rates for referred infants can be lower in rural areas. Thai parents should be aware that the first screening result is not a diagnosis and that same-day follow-up arrangements should be made if results are unclear.

## Warning Signs 🚨
- Baby does not startle to loud sounds by 1 month
- Baby is not calmed by your voice or does not recognize it by 2 months
- Baby does not turn toward sounds by 4 months
- No babbling sounds by 4–6 months
- Screen was not performed or referral was not followed up → request repeat screen immediately
