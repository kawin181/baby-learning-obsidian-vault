# Infant Reflux & GERD

## Summary
Gastroesophageal reflux (GER) — spitting up milk — is normal and affects up to two-thirds of healthy infants, peaking at 3–5 months and resolving on its own by 9–12 months. GERD is the pathological form, defined by pain, poor weight gain, or esophageal injury, and requires medical evaluation. AAP strongly recommends against routine acid-suppressing medications for simple reflux.

## What Research Says

### GER vs. GERD
**GER** (physiologic reflux) is normal: the lower esophageal sphincter (LES) is naturally weak in newborns, allowing stomach contents to flow back up. It occurs in up to 2/3 of healthy infants (AAP), starts around 2–3 weeks of age, peaks at 4–5 months, and resolves by 9–12 months as the LES matures.

**"Happy spitters"** are infants with GER who are comfortable and gaining weight well — no intervention needed beyond reassurance (AAP).

**GERD** = GER with consequences: persistent irritability, feeding refusal, back-arching, poor weight gain, or mucosal injury visible on endoscopy. NHS adds that **silent reflux** — where acid enters the esophagus without visible vomiting — can cause the same distress.

### Medications: Evidence of Overuse
AAP (2022 Pediatrics) and the Choosing Wisely campaign both recommend **against** routine use of acid suppressants (PPIs, H2-blockers) for infant reflux. Evidence shows these drugs are frequently over-prescribed, with risks including serious infections and bone fractures, and unclear benefit for physiologic GER. NHS also reserves pharmacological treatment (Gaviscon Infant, ranitidine, omeprazole) for confirmed GERD only.

## Practical Guidelines

1. **Observe first:** If the baby spits up but is content, growing, and wetting diapers normally → observation only, no treatment needed
2. **Upright position:** Keep baby upright during feeds and for 20–30 minutes after; helps gravity reduce reflux
3. **Smaller, more frequent feeds:** Reduces volume in stomach at each feed
4. **Formula adjustments (formula-fed):** Thickening agent or anti-reflux formula (with GP guidance)
5. **Breastfeeding:** Ensure good latch to minimize air swallowing; consider cow's milk protein elimination trial if persistent
6. **Never elevate cot head:** NHS explicitly advises against this — creates risk of baby sliding into unsafe position; safe sleep on flat back is paramount
7. **No acid suppressants without GP diagnosis of GERD:** These do not help happy spitters and carry real risks

## Thai Context
Thai families may receive advice from relatives to give gripe water, herbal teas, or traditional massage to address spitting-up. These are not evidence-based for reflux. Some older practices include placing babies prone (on stomach) to reduce reflux — this is dangerous and contradicts safe sleep guidelines. The correct approach remains upright positioning during/after feeds with back-sleeping for all sleep.

## Warning Signs 🚨
- Vomit is green, yellow, or contains blood → emergency evaluation (risk of bowel obstruction)
- Forceful projectile vomiting → same-day evaluation (rule out pyloric stenosis)
- Bloody or tarry stools
- Baby refuses feeds consistently and shows signs of dehydration (no wet nappies 12+ hours, sunken fontanelle)
- Poor weight gain or weight loss
- Back-arching, persistent screaming during or after every feed → GERD evaluation needed
- New onset of reflux after 6 months → warrants investigation
