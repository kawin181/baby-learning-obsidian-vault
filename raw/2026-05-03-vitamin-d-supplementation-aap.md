# Vitamin D Supplementation in Newborns — AAP HealthyChildren.org

## Key Points
- All breastfed infants need 400 IU (10 mcg) vitamin D per day starting within the first few days of life
- Formula-fed infants do NOT need supplementation if drinking >27 oz/day of fortified formula
- AAP doubled recommendation from 200 IU to 400 IU in 2008 based on growing evidence
- Vitamin D drops are the recommended delivery method for infants
- Deficiency causes rickets — bone-softening disease particularly dangerous in first 2 years
- Limited sun exposure (sunscreen, protective clothing) prevents natural synthesis

## Raw Content

### Who Needs It
- **Breastfed infants:** All breastfed babies, starting within first few days of life
- **Partially breastfed:** Also need supplementation
- **Formula-fed:** Only needed if consuming <27 oz per day of vitamin D-fortified formula
- Approximately 15% of children ages 1–11 have vitamin D deficiency (US data)

### Dose
- **0–12 months:** 400 IU (10 mcg) per day
- **12+ months:** 600 IU (15 mcg) per day
- Liquid vitamin D drops — best form for infants

### Why Breast Milk Is Insufficient
- Breast milk is naturally low in vitamin D
- Even mothers with adequate vitamin D levels cannot transfer enough through breast milk
- Sun exposure for infants: not recommended due to UV risk to infant skin
- Sunscreen use + indoor lifestyle in modern life reduces natural synthesis

### Deficiency Risks
- **Rickets:** Bone-softening disease causing bowed legs, soft skull, delayed motor development
- Increased fracture risk in older children
- Impaired immune function
- Long-term bone density loss

### How to Give
- Liquid drops — 400 IU in small volume
- Mix into formula or expressed breast milk, or give directly
- Keep out of reach of children
