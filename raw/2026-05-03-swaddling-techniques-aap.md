# Swaddling Techniques — AAP HealthyChildren

## Key Points
- Swaddling can calm infants and promote sleep when done correctly
- Always place swaddled baby on their BACK to sleep on a firm, flat surface
- Hip-healthy swaddling: legs must bend up and out freely — tight leg wrapping causes hip dysplasia
- Leave 2–3 finger widths of space between baby's chest and swaddle
- STOP swaddling when baby shows any sign of rolling over — as early as 2 months
- Never use weighted swaddles or weighted blankets
- Swaddling may decrease arousal — increasing SIDS risk if baby rolls prone
- Swaddling does NOT reduce the risk of SIDS — it must be paired with safe sleep practices
- Watch for overheating: sweating, flushed cheeks, damp hair, heat rash, rapid breathing

## Raw Content

### How to Swaddle (Step-by-Step)
1. Spread thin blanket flat, fold one corner down
2. Lay baby face-up with head above the folded corner
3. Straighten left arm, wrap left corner over body and tuck between right arm and body
4. Tuck right arm down, fold right corner over and tuck under left side
5. Loosely fold/twist bottom and tuck under one side
6. Check: hips move freely, at least 2–3 finger widths between chest and swaddle

### Hip Dysplasia Risk
Straightening baby's legs tightly during swaddling can cause hip dislocation or developmental dysplasia of the hip (DDH). The Pavlik position (legs bent up and out like a frog) is hip-healthy. Never swaddle legs straight and tight.

### SIDS Risk
Swaddling decreases infant arousal (ability to wake up). If a swaddled baby rolls to stomach, they cannot reposition and are at high risk of suffocation. Always monitor swaddled babies. Stop at first sign of rolling (can happen as early as 2 months).

### When to Stop
- Any attempt to roll over → stop swaddling immediately
- Typically around 3–4 months, but can be earlier
- Transition to a wearable sleep sack (arms free)
