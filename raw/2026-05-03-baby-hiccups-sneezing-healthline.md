# Baby Hiccups & Sneezing — Healthline / Cleveland Clinic

## Key Points
- Newborn sneezing is healthy and indicates proper nervous system function
- Primary cause: small nasal passages need frequent clearing of breast milk, mucus, and particles
- Sneezing is a reflex controlled by the nervous system — per Cleveland Clinic, "completely common for newborns"
- Sneezing helps babies learn nasal breathing
- CALL DOCTOR if sneezing + any of: cough, trouble breathing, feeding refusal, excessive tiredness, fever ≥100.4°F (38°C)
- For babies under 3 months: any fever ≥100.4°F requires URGENT medical attention — possible serious infection
- Rare: excessive sneezing (3–4 times in 30 min) can indicate neonatal abstinence syndrome (NAS) in babies born to mothers who used opioids
- Colored nasal discharge (yellow/green) indicates infection; clear discharge is normal
- Frequent sneezing alone is NOT a sign of illness

## Raw Content

### Newborn Sneezing — Normal
Newborns sneeze much more than older children because their nasal passages are small and sensitive. Sneezing clears:
- Residual amniotic fluid/mucus at birth
- Breast milk that entered the nasal passage
- Dust, dry air, temperature-related irritants
- Environmental particles during first exposure to the world

The Cleveland Clinic confirms this is "a reflex controlled by the nervous system" and "completely common."

### Newborn Sneezing — Concerning Signs
Contact pediatrician if sneezing accompanies:
- Cough
- Difficulty breathing (rapid breathing, flaring nostrils, retractions)
- Feeding refusal or inability to feed
- Excessive tiredness/lethargy
- Fever ≥100.4°F (38°C) — URGENT in any baby under 3 months

### Rare Concern: Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome (NAS)
In babies born to mothers who used opioids during pregnancy, excessive sneezing (3–4 times within 30 minutes) may be a sign of NAS. This is a rare but important clinical finding that should be noted by medical teams at delivery.

### Nasal Discharge Assessment
| Discharge Color | Significance |
|-----------------|--------------|
| Clear | Normal — viral or environmental irritation |
| Yellow/Green | Suggests bacterial infection |
| Blood-tinged | Requires medical evaluation |
