# Swaddling Techniques — NHS

## Key Points
- Swaddling helps settle young babies and promotes sleep
- Use thin materials only: muslin or cot sheet — NOT blankets (overheating risk)
- Head must remain UNCOVERED when swaddling
- Keep hips loose — tight hip wrapping strongly associated with developmental dysplasia of hip (DDH)
- Check temperature at back of neck — should be warm not hot
- Do not overdress under the swaddle
- Stop swaddling when baby shows signs of rolling over (~4 months)
- Do not swaddle if baby has a fever or infection
- Never put a swaddled baby to sleep on front or side — back only

## Raw Content

### Materials
Use thin, breathable fabrics only: muslin wraps or lightweight cot sheets. Avoid heavy blankets, fleece, or thick fabrics that trap heat. Overheating is a known risk factor for SIDS.

### Head Position
Always ensure the baby's head is uncovered. If the swaddle unravels and covers the face, the baby may suffocate. Check that the swaddle is secure and cannot come undone.

### Hip Safety
Swaddles must not be wrapped tightly around the hips. The hips should be free to spread apart and bend. Tight hip wrapping is strongly associated with developmental dysplasia of the hip (DDH). NHS recommends the "healthy hips" position throughout.

### Temperature Monitoring
Check the baby's temperature by placing a hand on the back of the neck (not hands or feet, which are normally cooler). If the neck is hot or sweaty, remove a layer. Keep the room at a comfortable temperature (16–20°C recommended).

### When to Stop
NHS advises stopping swaddling when the baby begins to show signs of rolling over, typically around 4 months. Once rolling begins, a swaddled baby who rolls prone cannot reposition and is at risk of suffocation.

### Safety Summary
- Back to sleep — always
- Thin fabric only
- Head uncovered
- Hips free to move
- Stop at first sign of rolling
