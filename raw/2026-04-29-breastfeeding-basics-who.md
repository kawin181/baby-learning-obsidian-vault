# Breastfeeding Basics — WHO

## Key Points
- WHO and UNICEF recommend **exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months** of life
- **Exclusive breastfeeding** = breast milk only; no other food or drink, not even water (except ORS, vitamins, minerals, medicines)
- **Initiate within 1 hour of birth** (early initiation is critical for establishing supply and providing colostrum)
- Continue breastfeeding alongside complementary foods for **2 years or beyond**
- Breast milk contains **all nutrients** an infant needs in the first 6 months
- Breastfeeding **protects against infections**: reduces risk of diarrhoea, pneumonia, and common childhood illnesses
- Breastfeeding **reduces risk of SIDS**, obesity, diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and certain cancers
- Breastfeeding **improves IQ** and is associated with higher income in adult life
- **Colostrum** (first milk, produced 0–3 days): thick, yellowish, high in antibodies (IgA) and immune factors; essential — do not discard

## Raw Content
### Why Exclusive Breastfeeding
Human milk is uniquely tailored to the infant's needs. It changes composition dynamically — colostrum in days 1–3, transitional milk 4–14 days, mature milk from ~2 weeks onward. No formula can replicate this adaptability.

### Immune Protection Mechanism
Breast milk contains:
- Secretory IgA — coats gut lining, blocks pathogens
- Lactoferrin — binds iron, inhibits bacterial growth
- Lysozyme — antibacterial enzyme
- Oligosaccharides — prebiotic, feeds healthy gut flora
- Living maternal immune cells — lymphocytes, macrophages

### Global Context
Only ~44% of infants 0–6 months globally are exclusively breastfed. Rates in Thailand are improving but remain below WHO targets. Cultural practices (giving honey, sugar water, or formula in the first days) interfere with breastfeeding establishment.

### For Fathers: Supporting Breastfeeding
- Ensure mother has adequate rest, nutrition, and fluids
- Help with household tasks so mother can focus on feeding
- Learn to recognize baby's hunger and satiety cues
- Skin-to-skin contact by fathers also promotes bonding and infant thermoregulation
- Encourage mother to seek lactation support early; do not suggest switching to formula unless medically indicated

### Benefits for Mother
- Reduces postpartum bleeding (oxytocin release)
- Reduces risk of breast and ovarian cancer
- Supports uterine involution
- May assist with postpartum weight loss
