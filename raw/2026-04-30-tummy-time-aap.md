# Tummy Time — AAP / HealthyChildren.org

## Key Points
- AAP recommends tummy time from day 1 home from hospital — start early
- Begin with 2–3 sessions/day, 3–5 minutes each
- Work up to 15–30 minutes total per day by 7 weeks of age
- Tummy time must be supervised and only when baby is awake
- Prevents flat head syndrome (positional plagiocephaly)
- Strengthens neck, shoulder, and core muscles needed for rolling, sitting, crawling
- "Back to Sleep, Tummy to Play" — sleep position rule doesn't change

## Raw Content
### Why Tummy Time Matters
- Since 1994 "Back to Sleep" campaign (to prevent SIDS), babies spend less time on their stomachs
- This means intentional tummy time during waking hours is more important than ever
- Tummy time builds the muscles that allow babies to hold their head up, roll over, sit, and eventually crawl

### AAP Official Recommendation
- Start from first day home from hospital
- Sessions: 2–3 times per day, starting at 3–5 minutes per session
- Gradually increase to 15–30 minutes spread throughout the day by 7 weeks
- Always supervised; only when baby is awake and alert
- Never during or right after a feed (risk of spit-up)

### Best Times for Tummy Time
- After a nap when baby is rested and calm
- After diaper change
- After bath
- Avoid right after feeding (30-minute wait recommended)

### Activities to Make Tummy Time Engaging
- Get on the floor face-to-face with baby — eye contact motivates
- Place bright toys or a mirror in front of baby
- Use a rolled towel or small tummy time pillow under baby's chest to ease the position
- "Chest to chest" position — parent lies back; baby lies chest-to-chest on parent (counts as tummy time)

### If Baby Hates Tummy Time
- Completely normal — many newborns resist initially
- Keep sessions brief and frequent; end before baby becomes very distressed
- Gradually increase duration as neck and shoulder muscles strengthen
- Position rolled towel under armpits to provide support
- Try tummy time on a slightly inclined surface initially
