# Infant Reflux & GERD — NHS

## Key Points
- Reflux = baby brings up milk during or shortly after feeding — very common
- Usually starts before 8 weeks old; usually better by 1 year
- Caused by weak lower esophageal sphincter (LES) — normal at this age
- Silent reflux = reflux without visible vomiting (but with other symptoms)
- Management: upright positioning, frequent burping, formula thickening if formula-fed
- Emergency signs: green/yellow/bloody vomit, projectile vomiting, bloody stools, high fever, feeding refusal

## Raw Content

### Definition & Cause
Reflux occurs when a baby brings up milk during or shortly after feeding. The lower esophageal sphincter (LES) is a muscular valve between the esophagus and stomach — in young infants it is naturally weak, allowing stomach contents to travel back up. This strengthens with development.

### Prevalence & Timeline
- Very common — affects the majority of newborns to some degree
- Usually starts before 8 weeks of age
- Usually resolves by 12 months as the LES strengthens

### Symptoms
- Spitting up or vomiting milk during/after feeds
- Coughing or hiccupping while feeding
- Restlessness and crying during feeds
- Swallowing or gulping after burping
- Inconsolable crying
- Poor weight gain (when milk is not retained)

**Silent reflux:** Infant shows signs of reflux (discomfort, feeding refusal, arching) without visible vomiting. Stomach acid still enters esophagus, causing irritation.

### Management
**Positioning:**
- Keep baby upright during feeds and for 20–30 minutes after
- Ensure safe sleep position (on back) is maintained — do NOT elevate cot head (NHS explicitly advises against this)

**Feeding adjustments:**
- Smaller, more frequent feeds
- For formula-fed: thickening powder or pre-thickened formula (Cow & Gate Comfort, Aptamil Anti-Reflux)
- For breastfed: ensure good latch to reduce air swallowing; consider cow's milk protein elimination if suspected allergy

**Medications (if recommended by GP):**
- Gaviscon Infant (alginate — thickens feed in stomach)
- Ranitidine or omeprazole — only for confirmed GERD with poor weight gain or esophagitis, not routine reflux

### When to Seek Medical Care
Routine GP appointment:
- Symptoms continue past 1 year old
- New reflux starting after 6 months
- Worry about weight gain

Same-day/emergency GP:
- Vomit is green or yellow, or contains blood
- Projectile vomiting (rule out pyloric stenosis)
- Bloody stools
- High fever with vomiting
- Baby refuses all feeds
- Signs of dehydration (dry mouth, sunken fontanelle, no wet nappies for 12 hours)
