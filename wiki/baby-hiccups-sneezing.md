# Baby Hiccups & Sneezing

## Summary
Both hiccups and sneezing are completely normal reflexes in newborns that alarm parents far more than they affect the baby. Hiccups are triggered by the immature diaphragm and feeding habits; sneezing is a nervous system reflex that keeps tiny nasal passages clear. Neither requires treatment on their own — but both carry specific warning signs that parents should know.

## What Research Says

### Hiccups

Hiccups in newborns are a normal reflex caused by sudden diaphragm contractions. The AAP notes that "most babies hiccup from time to time, and usually this bothers parents more than the infant." Newborns are also known to make a wide variety of sounds — grunts, squeaks, sighs, sneezes, and hiccups — as part of normal neurological development.

**Causes in newborns:**
- Overfeeding or gulping too much air during a frantic feed
- Swallowing air while crying before feeding
- Immature diaphragm that is still developing sphincter control
- Sudden temperature changes

**When hiccups typically occur:** Most often during or shortly after feeding.

### Sneezing

Newborn sneezing is a healthy, normal reflex controlled by the nervous system (Cleveland Clinic). Newborns sneeze much more frequently than older children because their nasal passages are very small and sensitive, requiring frequent clearing.

**What sneezing clears:**
- Residual amniotic fluid and mucus at birth
- Breast milk that enters the nasal passages during feeding
- Airborne dust, dry air, and temperature-related irritants
- Environmental particles during first exposure to the outside world

**Normal pattern:** Frequent sneezing, especially in the first weeks after birth, without accompanying symptoms.

## Practical Guidelines

### Managing Hiccups
| Situation | Action |
|-----------|--------|
| Hiccups during feeding | Pause, change position, try burping |
| Hiccups not resolving in 5–10 min | Resume feeding briefly — usually stops hiccups |
| Frequent hiccups | Feed before baby becomes extremely hungry; feed in calm environment |
| Baby unbothered by hiccups | Reassure parents — no intervention needed |

### Managing Sneezing
- Frequent sneezing alone: no action needed
- Dry air: consider room humidifier (without medicated additives)
- Clear nasal discharge: normal — gentle wipe with soft cloth if visible
- Saline nasal drops (pediatrician-recommended): can help if nasal congestion seems to interfere with feeding

### Preventing Hiccup-Triggering Overfeeding
- Bottle-fed: burp every 2–3 oz
- Breastfed: burp when switching breasts
- Use slow-flow nipples for bottle feeding to reduce gulping
- Feed on schedule before hunger escalates to frantic crying

## Warning Signs 🚨

### Hiccups — When to Call the Doctor
- Hiccups persist for hours (rare) and appear to cause significant distress
- Hiccups associated with arching of the back during/after feeds (may suggest GERD)
- Hiccups significantly interfere with feeding frequency or duration

### Sneezing — When to Call the Doctor (URGENT)
- **Any fever ≥100.4°F (38°C) in a baby under 3 months** → go to ER/call doctor immediately
- Sneezing + cough + difficulty breathing (rapid, labored, nostrils flaring, retractions)
- Sneezing + feeding refusal or inability to latch
- Sneezing + excessive lethargy (hard to wake, unresponsive)
- Yellow, green, or blood-tinged nasal discharge
- Excessive sneezing (3–4 times in 30 minutes) with no obvious cause → flag for NAS screening if relevant history
