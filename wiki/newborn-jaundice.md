# Newborn Jaundice

## Summary
More than 80% of newborns develop some degree of jaundice (ตัวเหลือง) — yellowing of skin and eyes caused by elevated bilirubin from normal fetal red blood cell breakdown. Most cases are physiologic and resolve without treatment within 1–2 weeks. Severe untreated hyperbilirubinemia can cause permanent brain damage (kernicterus); early monitoring and treatment when needed prevents this outcome.

## What Research Says
**Physiology:** At birth, fetal hemoglobin (HbF) is replaced by adult hemoglobin. The breakdown produces unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin, which the immature neonatal liver cannot clear efficiently. Bilirubin deposits in the skin and eyes, causing yellow discoloration.

**Timing helps classify jaundice:**
- Appears **<24 hours**: always pathologic — blood type incompatibility or hemolytic disease; urgent evaluation
- Appears **day 2–3**: likely physiologic; peaks day 3–5; resolves by 1–2 weeks in term infants
- Persists **>2 weeks**: investigate further (hypothyroidism, biliary atresia); breast milk jaundice is common benign cause persisting up to 3 months

**AAP 2022 revised guideline** raised thresholds for phototherapy initiation based on newer evidence that bilirubin is not neurotoxic at levels previously treated. Risk is now stratified by: TSB (total serum bilirubin) level, age in hours, and neurotoxicity risk factors.

**Neurotoxicity risk factors (require lower treatment threshold):**
- Isoimmune hemolytic disease (ABO or Rh incompatibility)
- G6PD deficiency
- Sepsis, acidosis, asphyxia
- Albumin <3 g/dL

**G6PD deficiency and Thai population:** G6PD deficiency is X-linked recessive and prevalent in Thailand (incidence up to 10–15% in some regions). Affected infants can develop rapid, severe, unpredictable bilirubin spikes. Trigger substances (naphthalene/mothballs, certain medications, fava beans) must be avoided. Universal newborn screening for G6PD is part of Thailand's national screening program (โครงการคัดกรองทารกแรกเกิด).

**Breastfeeding and jaundice:**
- *Breastfeeding jaundice* (days 2–5): insufficient milk intake → fewer stools → less bilirubin excreted. Treat by improving latch and feeding frequency — 8–12 feeds/day lowers bilirubin
- *Breast milk jaundice* (weeks 1–3+): substances in mature milk reduce bilirubin metabolism. Usually benign; do NOT stop breastfeeding. Rarely requires phototherapy.

**Phototherapy:** Blue-green light (450–490 nm) converts bilirubin in skin to water-soluble isomers excreted without liver conjugation. Eyes must be shielded. Feeding continues during phototherapy breaks.

**Exchange transfusion:** Reserved for severe hyperbilirubinemia unresponsive to intensive phototherapy, especially in hemolytic disease. Rapidly removes bilirubin and maternal antibodies.

## Practical Guidelines
**What to check at home (days 2–5):**
1. Check baby in natural light (near a window)
2. Press gently on forehead, nose, or chest — if the skin looks yellow when released, jaundice is present
3. Jaundice progresses head to toe: face → chest → abdomen → legs/feet
4. Jaundice on legs and feet = likely significant; see a doctor
5. Ensure baby is feeding 8–12 times/day; adequate stooling (yellow-green stools) moves bilirubin out

**Medical follow-up:**
- All newborns should have bilirubin (TSB or TcB) measured at ≥24 hours or before discharge
- Follow-up visit at **3–5 days of life** for bilirubin check and weight assessment
- Keep doctor's follow-up appointments — the first 5–7 days are the highest-risk window

**Under phototherapy:**
- Do not cover the baby with blankets (skin exposure needed for effect)
- Eyes must be shielded at all times under light
- Continue breastfeeding — bring baby out for feeds every 2–3 hours
- Monitor wet diapers and stool output

## Thai Context
ตัวเหลืองในทารกแรกเกิดเป็นปัญหาที่พบบ่อยมากในประเทศไทย โดยเฉพาะในทารกที่มีภาวะพร่องเอนไซม์ G6PD ซึ่งพบได้ถึง 10–15% ของประชากรไทยบางพื้นที่ ขอให้ระวังเรื่องลูกแดงใกล้ตัวเด็ก (ลูกเหม็นหรือการบูร/นาฟทาลีน) ซึ่งสามารถกระตุ้นให้เกิดตัวเหลืองรุนแรงในเด็กที่มีภาวะพร่องเอนไซม์ G6PD ได้ การตรวจคัดกรอง G6PD เป็นส่วนหนึ่งของโครงการคัดกรองทารกแรกเกิดของกระทรวงสาธารณสุขไทย ควรแจ้งผลให้แพทย์ทราบก่อนออกจากโรงพยาบาล

Traditional use of naphthalene mothballs (ลูกแดง/การบูร) near newborns is a significant risk trigger for G6PD-deficient infants and must be strictly avoided. Cultural practices involving camphor-based products should be discussed explicitly with extended family.

## Warning Signs 🚨
**Go to the emergency room immediately if baby has:**
- Extreme limpness, floppiness, or difficulty waking
- High-pitched, unusual cry
- Backward arching of the head and body (opisthotonus)
- Fever
- Jaundice appearing in the first 24 hours of life

**See a doctor promptly (same day) if:**
- Yellow color reaches legs and soles of feet
- Jaundice persists beyond 3 weeks in formula-fed babies or 6 weeks in breastfed babies
- Baby is feeding poorly or losing weight along with jaundice
- Urine is dark yellow or stools are very pale/white (suggests liver problem)
