# Baby Nail Care — AAP (HealthyChildren.org)

## Key Points
- Newborn fingernails grow ~0.1 mm/day — trim or file about once a week
- Best time: trim when baby is asleep (hands less likely to move)
- Technique: push down fingertip skin → clip/trim both sides → file edges smooth
- Tools: baby nail clippers, small round-ended safety scissors, or emery board
- Do NOT bite or peel nails — infection risk
- Mittens: AAP says "rarely needed" — regular nail trimming is better
- If using mittens, limit to sleep time only so baby can explore with hands when awake
- Toenails: same principles, but toenails grow slower than fingernails

## Raw Content

### Growth Rate
Fingernails grow ~0.1 mm per day — faster in young infants, in males, and in summer. Weekly trimming is usually needed.

### Tools
- **Baby nail clippers** — most controlled option
- **Small round-ended safety scissors** — good for nervous parents
- **Fine emery board** — gentlest option, zero cut risk; takes longer
- **Avoid:** adult clippers/scissors (too large), biting/peeling nails (introduces mouth bacteria → paronychia risk)

### Technique (Step by Step)
1. Wait until baby is deeply asleep
2. Hold baby's hand firmly but gently
3. Push down the fingertip pad to expose the full nail
4. Clip or trim both sides of the nail — follow the natural curve
5. Smooth any sharp edges with emery board
6. For toenails: same technique; trim straight across (not curved) to prevent ingrown nails

### Timing
- During sleep = safest (baby won't jerk hand)
- If baby tends to be still while feeding, another parent can trim during feeds

### Mittens (AAP Position)
- "Mittens are rarely needed for newborns" — Dr. Natasha Burgert, AAP
- Regular nail trimming eliminates the need for mittens
- If parents use mittens, limit to sleep time — babies need free hands to explore and develop fine motor awareness
- Bluish/cool hands are normal in newborns — NOT a reason to add mittens

### When to Seek Help
- Accidental cut that bleeds: apply gentle pressure with clean cloth, typically resolves in minutes
- If red, swollen, or discharge around nail bed → may be paronychia (nail infection) → see pediatrician
