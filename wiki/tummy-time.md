# Tummy Time

## Summary
Tummy time — placing an awake, supervised baby on their stomach — is the single most important physical activity for 0–3 month olds. It builds the neck, shoulder, and core muscles needed for all future motor milestones, and counterbalances the flat-back-head risk from safe-sleep back positioning. Start from day one and build gradually to 30 minutes daily by 7 weeks.

## What Research Says
The AAP's systematic review (Pediatrics, 2020) confirms that tummy time improves motor outcomes and reduces the risk of positional plagiocephaly (flat head syndrome). Since the 1994 "Back to Sleep" campaign dramatically reduced SIDS deaths, tummy time during awake hours has become more important to compensate for decreased prone time overall.

**Key developmental milestones enabled by tummy time:**
- 1 month: briefly lifts head
- 2 months: holds head up momentarily; smoother head control
- 3 months (Mayo Clinic): lifts head and chest supported by elbows — "mini push-up" position; head wobble mostly resolved

**Minimum dose:** AAP recommends 15–30 minutes total per day by 7 weeks, spread across multiple short sessions. NHS recommends at least 30 minutes/day for non-mobile infants.

## Practical Guidelines

### Getting Started
- **Start immediately**: begin from first day home from hospital (or even day one in hospital)
- First sessions: 2–3 times/day, 3–5 minutes each
- Build to 15–30 minutes/day spread throughout the day by 7 weeks
- Always awake, always supervised — never leave baby unattended on tummy

### Best Times to Do Tummy Time
- After a nap (baby rested and calm)
- After a diaper change
- After a bath
- Wait at least 20–30 minutes after feeding (reduces spit-up risk)

### Positions
1. **Floor mat** — firm, flat surface; place baby prone; get down face-to-face with baby
2. **Chest-to-chest** — parent reclines; baby lies chest-to-chest on parent (counts as tummy time); parent must be wide awake
3. **Lap tummy time** — baby prone across parent's thighs; head slightly higher than chest

### Making It Easier (If Baby Resists)
- Roll a small towel and place under armpits/chest to prop slightly — reduces strain
- Place a baby-safe mirror or bright toy at baby's eye level
- Get down on the floor and make eye contact — social motivation works
- Try tummy time on a slightly inclined surface (e.g., nursing pillow) as a transition step
- Keep sessions short; end before baby becomes very distressed
- Consistency matters more than duration — daily practice builds tolerance

### Safety Rules
- Tummy time = awake and supervised ONLY
- Never use tummy time as a sleep position (SIDS risk — see [[wiki/newborn-safe-sleep]])
- Avoid right after feeding
- Stop if baby seems in significant distress and try again later

## Warning Signs 🚨
- Baby consistently unable to turn head to either side by 2 months → may indicate torticollis (tight neck muscle); see a pediatrician
- No head lifting at all by 3 months → mention to pediatrician at well-child visit
- Significant asymmetry in head shape developing → discuss positional plagiocephaly management with pediatrician
- Baby cries inconsolably every time → rule out reflux or other discomfort; discuss with doctor
