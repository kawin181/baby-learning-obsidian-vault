# Baby Hiccups & Sneezing — AAP HealthyChildren

## Key Points
- Hiccups are normal in infants — they bother parents more than babies
- Newborns commonly make a wide variety of sounds: grunts, squeaks, sighs, sneezes, and hiccups
- If hiccups occur during feeding: change position, try burping, help baby relax
- If hiccups persist >5–10 minutes, try resuming feeding — this usually stops them
- Prevention: feed baby when calm and before extremely hungry
- Sneezing is normal and a sign the respiratory/nervous system is working
- Burp bottle-fed infants every 2–3 oz; burp breastfed infants when switching breasts
- Spit-up is generally harmless; always keep baby on back for sleep even with frequent spit-ups

## Raw Content

### Hiccups
Hiccups are a very common occurrence in infants. AAP notes that hiccups typically concern parents more than the baby. They are part of normal newborn behavior, alongside grunts, squeaks, sighs, and sneezes.

**Management during feeding:**
1. Change baby's position
2. Try to get baby to burp
3. Help baby relax
4. Wait until hiccups are gone before resuming feeding
5. If not resolved in 5–10 minutes, resume feeding — this usually stops them

**Prevention:** Feed baby when calm and before they are extremely hungry. Gulping air during a frantic feed is a common trigger.

### Newborn Sounds (Context)
Newborns make diverse sounds as part of normal development. Parents should be reassured that most grunts, sneezes, and hiccups are benign reflexes.

### Related: Burping & Spit-Up
- Bottle-fed: burp every 2–3 oz
- Breastfed: burp at breast switch
- Spit-up: normal, generally harmless. Safe sleep position (back) remains the same regardless of spit-up frequency
