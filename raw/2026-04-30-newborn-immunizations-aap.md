# Newborn Immunizations — AAP / HealthyChildren.org

## Key Points
- AAP releases evidence-based 2026 immunization schedule (updated annually)
- Hepatitis B (HepB): first dose within 24 hours of birth — extremely important
- Vaccines at 2 months: DTaP, IPV, Hib, PCV15/20, Rotavirus, HepB (2nd dose)
- RSV immunization: for infants <8 months during RSV season (fall–spring)
- Vaccine schedule is designed to protect at the most vulnerable period — early immunity
- No credible evidence that vaccines cause autism (extensively studied)

## Raw Content
### Why Vaccinate So Early?
- Young infants are most vulnerable to certain diseases (pertussis, meningitis)
- Some maternal antibodies wane by 2 months, leaving the baby unprotected
- The immune system of a 2-month-old is fully capable of responding to multiple vaccines simultaneously
- Delaying vaccines = extended window of vulnerability

### 2026 AAP/CDC Recommended Schedule (0–3 months)

| Age | Vaccines |
|-----|----------|
| Birth (within 24h) | HepB (dose 1) |
| 1–2 months | HepB (dose 2) |
| 2 months | DTaP (dose 1), IPV (dose 1), Hib (dose 1), PCV15 or PCV20 (dose 1), Rotavirus (dose 1) |
| RSV season (Oct–Mar, <8 months) | Nirsevimab (RSV immunization) |

### Key Vaccines Explained
- **HepB (Hepatitis B)**: Prevents liver disease and liver cancer; transmitted from mother if infected; high-risk period is birth
- **DTaP**: Diphtheria, Tetanus, Pertussis (whooping cough) — pertussis is most dangerous for young infants
- **IPV**: Inactivated Polio Vaccine
- **Hib**: Haemophilus influenzae type b — cause of bacterial meningitis
- **PCV**: Pneumococcal Conjugate Vaccine — prevents pneumonia, meningitis
- **Rotavirus**: Oral vaccine; prevents severe diarrhea/dehydration

### Common Parent Concerns
- **"Too many too soon"**: Schedule is carefully designed; immune system handles thousands of antigens daily
- **Pain/fever**: Normal reaction; give acetaminophen (Tylenol) if needed; NOT aspirin
- **"My baby is premature"**: Preterm babies should still receive vaccines on schedule (by chronological age, not corrected age) — they are more vulnerable, not less
