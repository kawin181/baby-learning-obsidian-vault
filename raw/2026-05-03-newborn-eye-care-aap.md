# Newborn Eye Care & Blocked Tear Ducts — AAP HealthyChildren.org

## Key Points
- Blocked tear duct (nasolacrimal duct obstruction) affects approximately 10% of newborns
- Both sides blocked in 30% of affected cases
- Over 90% of blocked tear ducts open on their own by 12 months of age
- Infection (conjunctivitis with pus) is the main complication — treat with antibiotic eye drops
- Lacrimal sac massage is optional; the duct will open without massage in most cases, but some doctors recommend it
- Children over 12 months who still have blockage should be referred to an ophthalmologist — probing may be needed

## Raw Content

### Prevalence
10% of newborns are born with a blocked tear duct. Both sides are blocked in 30% of affected babies.

### Natural History
- Over 90% of blocked tear ducts resolve spontaneously by 12 months
- No treatment is required unless the duct becomes infected

### Symptoms
- Watery eye (tears pool at corners or spill down cheek)
- Sticky or crusty discharge on eyelid upon waking (not infection, just stagnant tears)
- Eye may look moist or glassy

### Infection Signs
- Pus discharge (yellow-green) from the eye
- Eyelids stuck together
- Red, inflamed eyelids
- These indicate secondary conjunctivitis → needs antibiotic eye drops

### Massage (Optional)
- AAP notes compression of the lacrimal sac is not required; duct opens without it
- Some doctors suggest gentle compression/massage of the area where tears collect (inner corner of eye)
- Parents should confirm technique with their pediatrician before starting

### Escalation
- If blockage persists past 12 months → referral to pediatric ophthalmologist
- Probing procedure (outpatient, done under brief sedation) opens the duct mechanically
- Rarely, surgery is needed
