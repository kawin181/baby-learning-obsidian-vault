# Newborn Safe Sleep

## Summary
Sleep-related infant deaths account for approximately 3,500 deaths per year in the US. Both the AAP (2022) and NHS recommend placing babies on their back, alone, on a firm flat surface in the same room as parents for at least the first 6 months. Following these guidelines reduces the risk of SIDS by up to 50%.

## What Research Says
The AAP 2022 updated guidelines and NHS guidance are in strong alignment on safe sleep principles:

**Back to sleep:** Supine (back) positioning for every sleep reduces SIDS risk substantially compared to prone or side positions. The supine position keeps the airway open and allows the baby to arouse more easily from deep sleep.

**Firm, flat, separate surface:** The American Academy of Pediatrics specifies that sleep surfaces inclined more than 10° from horizontal are unsafe for infant sleep. In-bed sleepers, nest/pod products, and inclined bouncers/rockers are not safe for unsupervised sleep.

**Room-sharing reduces SIDS by ~50%:** Keeping baby in the same room (but on a separate surface) significantly reduces SIDS risk, especially in the first 6 months. Bed-sharing on a safe sleep surface is distinct from co-sleeping on sofas or armchairs, which carry the highest risk.

**No soft bedding:** Pillows, loose blankets, bumpers, soft toys, and positioners have all been associated with suffocation deaths. Baby sleeping bags fitted correctly around the shoulders are safer than loose blankets.

**Temperature 16–20 °C:** Overheating is a modifiable SIDS risk factor. Dress baby in one more layer than an adult in the same environment.

**Breastfeeding is protective:** Any amount of breastfeeding reduces SIDS risk; exclusive breastfeeding is most protective.

**Pacifiers at sleep onset:** Evidence supports a SIDS-protective effect. Offer at every sleep after breastfeeding is established (~3–4 weeks); does not need reinsertion after baby sleeps.

**Smoke-free environment:** Both prenatal and postnatal smoke exposure are among the strongest SIDS risk factors. No one should smoke anywhere near the baby.

**Tummy time when awake:** Essential for motor development and to prevent positional plagiocephaly. Start day 1, build to 15–30 min/day total by 7 weeks of age.

## Practical Guidelines
**What fathers should set up before baby comes home:**
1. Set up a firm-mattress crib or bassinet in the parents' bedroom — no pillows, bumpers, or loose items
2. Buy a room thermometer; set HVAC to maintain 16–20 °C at night
3. Use fitted sheets only; consider a baby sleeping bag (appropriately sized)
4. Have smoke-free conversations with all household members and visitors before birth
5. Learn and model the "back to sleep" habit from day one

**Daily checklist:**
- [ ] Baby placed on back for every sleep
- [ ] Sleep surface is firm and flat
- [ ] No soft objects in the sleep area
- [ ] Baby is in the room (not in the bed)
- [ ] Room temperature is comfortable (feel back of neck — warm, not sweaty)

## Thai Context
Thai traditional practices such as placing babies on their stomachs to sleep (นอนคว่ำ) or sleeping in the family bed on soft mattresses are common but increase SIDS risk. It is important to discuss safe sleep with extended family members (grandparents, aunts/uncles) who may be the primary caregivers. Thai maternal and child health guidelines (กรมอนามัย) align with WHO/AAP recommendations on back-to-sleep positioning.

## Warning Signs 🚨
Seek immediate emergency care if:
- Baby has stopped breathing or is blue/pale around the lips
- Baby is unresponsive or cannot be roused
- Baby makes gasping sounds during sleep regularly

Contact a doctor if:
- Baby consistently turns to one side during sleep (may indicate torticollis affecting positioning)
- You notice flattening of one side of the head (positional plagiocephaly)
