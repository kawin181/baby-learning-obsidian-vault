# Newborn Sleep Patterns & Schedules

## Summary
Newborns sleep 8–18 hours per day in short 1–2 hour bursts, with no predictable schedule for the first 3–4 months. Regular sleep cycles and circadian rhythms are not yet developed — babies wake frequently because their stomachs are small, not because something is wrong. A consistent simple bedtime routine and clear day/night signals help the brain clock develop gradually.

## What Research Says
- **Total daily sleep**: AAP reports 16–17 hours/day; NHS notes the normal range is 8–18 hours — individual variation is wide and normal.
- **Sleep cycle length**: Infant sleep cycles are ~45–50 minutes (vs adult 90 min). Waking between cycles is biologically normal, not a "sleep problem."
- **Circadian rhythm**: Newborns have no body clock at birth. The circadian rhythm develops gradually, typically showing early consolidation by 3 months and more regularity by 4–6 months.
- **Night waking**: Expected until at least 3–6 months. A Lullaby Trust survey found 59% of parents with babies under 12 months report fewer than 4 continuous hours of sleep.
- **Milestone**: By 3–6 months, some babies extend nighttime stretches to 5–8 hours as stomach capacity increases and feeds reduce.

## Practical Guidelines
1. **Day/night training from birth**: Bright light, normal noise, and engagement during the day. Dim lights, quiet voices, and minimal stimulation during night feeds and nappy changes.
2. **Simple bedtime routine**: Start early — bath (or wipe-down), fresh nappy, dim lights, quiet story or song, cuddle. Consistency over perfection.
3. **Room-sharing, not bed-sharing**: Keep baby in the same room for at least 6 months. Separate sleep surface (cot, Moses basket).
4. **Safe sleep position**: Always back-to-sleep on a flat, firm, non-inclined surface.
5. **Normal noise is fine**: Background household noise helps babies learn to sleep through sound — silence is not required.
6. **Feed first**: Night waking in 0–3 months almost always means hunger. Respond promptly; sleep training is not appropriate at this age.
7. **Watch for sleep cues**: Yawning, eye-rubbing, zoning out, fussiness — these indicate readiness for sleep. Put baby down before overtired.
8. **Share the night load**: If breastfeeding, partner can give one expressed milk feed at night to allow feeding parent a longer sleep stretch.

## Thai Context
Thai cultural practice often involves co-sleeping (นอนร่วมเตียง), which increases SIDS risk particularly if parents smoke or have consumed alcohol. Room-sharing with a separate surface (เตียงลูก/Moses basket ข้างเตียง) offers the same comfort benefit with much lower risk. Grandparent care during daytime naps is common — reinforce day/night routine consistency across caregivers.

## Warning Signs 🚨
- Baby sleeping more than 5 hours in a row in the first 2 weeks without waking for feeds (may indicate feeding problem or illness)
- Cannot be roused from sleep / unusually limp
- Sleeping far fewer than 8 hours in 24 hours alongside poor feeding and weight gain
- Snoring, noisy breathing, or prolonged pauses in breathing during sleep
