# Flat Head Syndrome (Positional Plagiocephaly) — AAP (HealthyChildren.org)

## Key Points
- Positional skull deformity affects approximately 20% of infants
- Typically develops in the first 4–12 weeks of life
- 85% of affected babies also have torticollis (tight neck muscle on one side)
- Head-shaping pillows are NOT recommended — FDA warns of suffocation risk
- Tummy time goal: gradually increase to 1 hour daily in short supervised intervals
- Helmet therapy considered only for moderate-to-severe cases unresponsive to repositioning by 5–6 months
- Back sleeping for safety must NOT be changed — work on positioning during awake time

## Raw Content

### What It Is
Positional skull deformity (plagiocephaly) occurs when babies spend extended time in one position, causing the still-soft skull to flatten. The condition has increased since the "Back to Sleep" campaign for SIDS prevention, as babies now spend more time on their backs.

### Causes
- Preferred sleeping position (baby always turning head one direction)
- Insufficient supervised tummy time during waking hours
- Multiple births (cramped womb positioning)
- Prematurity and prolonged back-lying in NICU
- Birth complications or delivery instruments (forceps/vacuum)
- Torticollis — tight sternocleidomastoid muscle causing head tilt; present in ~85% of cases

### Prevention Strategies
- Begin supervised tummy time from birth
- Gradually increase tummy time to 1 hour daily across short sessions
- Limit time in car seats, bouncers, and swings (all keep head in same position)
- Alternate arms when holding and feeding infant
- Rotate which end of crib baby's head is placed — changes visual stimulus angle
- Place baby slightly to alternate sides for sleep (not full side — keep on back for SIDS safety)

### Treatment Options
1. **Repositioning therapy** (first-line): most effective if started early
2. **Physical therapy**: evaluates for motor delays, torticollis; teaches stretching exercises
3. **Helmet therapy (cranial orthosis)**: for moderate-to-severe flattening unresponsive to positioning by age 5–6 months; most effective when started before 12 months

### When to Contact Pediatrician
- Head appears unevenly flat on one side
- Ears seem misaligned
- Forehead has parallelogram asymmetry
- Baby cannot turn head equally both directions (torticollis sign)
