# Vitamin D Supplementation in Newborns

## Summary
Both the AAP and NHS recommend that all breastfed infants receive 400 IU (10 mcg) of vitamin D daily beginning within the first few days of life. Breast milk alone does not contain adequate vitamin D, and sun exposure is not a safe or reliable source for newborns. Formula-fed babies who consume more than 27 oz (AAP) or 500 ml (NHS) of fortified formula per day do not need extra supplementation.

## What Research Says
- **Why breast milk is insufficient:** Even mothers with good vitamin D status cannot transfer enough through breast milk to meet infant needs. Breast milk averages only 15–40 IU of vitamin D per liter — far below the 400 IU daily requirement (AAP).
- **Rickets risk:** Vitamin D deficiency is the primary cause of nutritional rickets — a bone-softening disease that causes bowed legs, soft skull bones, and delayed motor development, with greatest risk in the first 2 years (AAP, 2008 clinical report).
- **Dose increase:** AAP doubled its recommendation from 200 IU to 400 IU in 2008 based on updated evidence of deficiency prevalence (~15% of U.S. children ages 1–11 are deficient).
- **Sun exposure not sufficient:** Modern indoor lifestyles, sunscreen use, and UV risk to infant skin make sun exposure an unreliable and unsafe source (AAP, NHS).
- **Formula adequacy threshold:** Formula is fortified at 40 IU/100 ml (UK) / ~67 IU per 8 oz (US). At >500 ml/day (NHS) or >27 oz/day (AAP), formula alone provides sufficient vitamin D.

## Practical Guidelines

### Dosing
| Age | Dose |
|-----|------|
| 0–12 months (breastfed) | 400 IU (10 mcg) per day |
| 0–12 months (formula-fed) | Not needed if >500 ml/day fortified formula |
| 12 months–4 years | 600 IU (15 mcg) per day |

### When to Start
- Begin within the **first few days of life** for all breastfed infants (AAP)
- NHS: from birth, regardless of mother's own vitamin D status

### How to Give
- **Liquid vitamin D drops** — small volume, easy to give
- Drop onto a clean spoon aimed at the inside of the cheek
- Can drop onto nipple/breast before feeding
- Can mix into expressed breast milk or formula
- **Never combine with cod liver oil** — cod liver oil also contains vitamins A and D, creating overdose risk (NHS)

### Formula-Fed Babies
- No supplementation needed once consuming >500 ml (1 pint) or >27 oz per day of vitamin D-fortified formula
- Partially formula-fed: supplement the difference if total formula intake is below threshold

## Thai Context
Thailand has high year-round sun intensity, which may lead to assumptions that vitamin D deficiency is rare. However, indoor lifestyles, air conditioning, SPF use, and clothing covering practices mean many urban Thai newborns do not get adequate sun. No specific Thai Ministry of Health guideline found, but AAP/NHS recommendations are applicable. สมาคมกุมารแพทย์ไทย (Pediatric Society of Thailand) follows international guidelines; parents should ask their pediatrician for specific product recommendations available in Thailand.

## Warning Signs 🚨
- **Signs of rickets** (severe deficiency): bowed legs, soft skull (craniotabes), tender bones, delayed sitting/walking, wide wrists
- **Signs of vitamin D overdose** (rare but possible with excessive supplementing): nausea, vomiting, weakness, frequent urination, constipation, elevated calcium — seek medical attention immediately
- Never exceed the recommended dose
- If using cod liver oil: **do not add additional vitamin D drops**
