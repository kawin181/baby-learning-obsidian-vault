# Breastfeeding Basics

## Summary
Both WHO and AAP recommend exclusive breastfeeding for the first 6 months of life. Breast milk provides complete nutrition, immune protection, and reduces risks of SIDS, infections, obesity, and chronic disease. A proper latch and feeding 8–12 times per 24 hours are the two most critical factors for establishing successful breastfeeding.

## What Research Says
**WHO/UNICEF recommendation:** Exclusive breastfeeding for 6 months, then continued alongside complementary foods for 2 years or beyond. "Exclusive" means no water, formula, or other food — only breast milk.

**AAP 2022 policy statement:** Aligns with WHO; any amount of breastfeeding confers health benefits. Breastfeeding reduces SIDS risk and is one of the AAP's safe sleep recommendations.

**Feeding frequency:** 8–12 times per 24 hours in the newborn period (roughly every 2–3 hours). Frequent feeding establishes and maintains milk supply through supply-demand feedback. Breastfeeding 9–10 times per day is also associated with lower bilirubin levels (reducing jaundice risk).

**Colostrum is critical:** The thick, yellowish first milk produced in the first 1–3 days is low in volume but very high in antibodies (especially secretory IgA), immune factors, and growth factors. It acts as the baby's first vaccine — do not discard or supplement with formula unless medically necessary.

**Latch quality determines success:** Poor latch is the most common cause of nipple pain, low milk intake, and early breastfeeding discontinuation. A correct latch means the baby takes in a significant portion of the areola, not just the nipple.

**Immune components of breast milk:** Secretory IgA (coats gut lining), lactoferrin (antibacterial), lysozyme (antibacterial), oligosaccharides (prebiotic for gut flora), and living maternal immune cells. These cannot be replicated by formula.

## Practical Guidelines
**Latch technique (step by step):**
1. Get comfortable — back support, pillow under arm if needed
2. Hold baby tummy-to-tummy; ear-shoulder-hip in a straight line
3. Position baby's **nose to nipple** (not mouth to nipple) — encourages wide gape
4. Wait for a wide, yawn-like open mouth
5. Bring baby to breast quickly; baby's chin contacts breast first
6. Check: lower lip is well below nipple, upper lip is above, nose near but not buried in breast, lips flanged outward (fish-lips)

**Good signs of effective feeding:**
- Rhythmic suck-swallow pattern, audible swallowing
- Breast softens during feeding
- Baby releases breast spontaneously when satisfied
- After feeding: nipple is round, not flattened or pinched

**Monitoring adequate intake (first 2 weeks):**
- Days 1–2: 1–2 wet diapers/day, meconium stools
- Days 3–4: 3–4 wet diapers/day, stool starts to turn green/yellow
- Day 5+: **6+ wet diapers and 3+ stools per day**
- Birth weight regained by **10–14 days**
- Weight loss up to 7–10% in first days is normal; >10% needs evaluation

**Common holds:**
- **Cross-cradle**: ideal for newborns — gives head control
- **Football/clutch hold**: after C-section or with large breasts
- **Laid-back/biological nurturing**: gravity helps deep latch; good in first days

**Father's role:**
- Create a quiet, calm environment for feeding
- Bring water and snacks to mother during sessions (breastfeeding is calorie-intensive)
- Take over diaper changes, bathing, and burping after feeds
- Learn hunger cues alongside mother
- Do NOT suggest formula unless clinically indicated — this undermines supply confidence
- Skin-to-skin contact by fathers (kangaroo care) also stabilizes baby's temperature and promotes bonding

## Thai Context
In Thailand, traditional beliefs sometimes encourage giving newborns sugar water, boiled rice water (น้ำข้าว), or honey in the first days — all of which interfere with breastfeeding establishment and colostrum intake. Honey carries a risk of infant botulism and should never be given to infants under 12 months. Thai grandmothers (ยาย/ย่า) often have strong opinions; fathers can play a key role in diplomatically advocating for exclusive breastfeeding. กรมอนามัย and the Thai Pediatric Society support WHO exclusive breastfeeding guidelines.

## Warning Signs 🚨
Contact a lactation consultant or doctor if:
- Nipple pain persists beyond the first 30 seconds of a feed
- Nipple is "lipstick-shaped" or creased/cracked after feeding (indicates shallow latch)
- Baby regains weight slowly or loses >10% of birth weight
- Fewer than 6 wet diapers/day after day 5
- Baby is inconsolable after feeds or appears to be always hungry

Seek urgent care if:
- Breast becomes red, hard, hot, and painful with fever (mastitis)
- Baby shows signs of dehydration: sunken fontanelle, no tears, dark urine, extreme lethargy
