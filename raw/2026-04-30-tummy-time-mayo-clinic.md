# Tummy Time — Mayo Clinic

## Key Points
- Tummy time is essential for developing neck, arm, and back muscle control
- By end of month 3: most babies can lift head and chest, supported by elbows, while on tummy
- "Little and often" — short frequent sessions are better than one long session
- Start from birth on parent's chest (chest-to-chest counts)
- Minimum 30 minutes of tummy time per day spread throughout the day (for non-mobile infants)
- Reduces risk of flat-head (positional plagiocephaly) at back or side of skull

## Raw Content
### Developmental Context
- Birth to 3 months is critical window for building foundational motor strength
- Tummy time directly trains: neck extensors, shoulder stabilizers, core muscles
- These muscles enable: head control → rolling → sitting → crawling

### Mayo Clinic Developmental Milestones (Tummy Time related)
- **1 month**: can briefly lift head while on stomach
- **2 months**: holds head up momentarily; smoother head movements
- **3 months**: lifts head and chest off surface, supported by elbows; "mini push-up" position; head wobble mostly gone

### How to Start
- From birth: lay baby on your chest while you recline — baby is on their tummy, gets proprioceptive input
- Progress to flat floor mat around 2–3 weeks if tolerated
- "Little and often" principle: start 1–2 minutes, increase as tolerated

### Managing Resistance
- Many newborns get fussy or cry during tummy time — expected initially
- Keep sessions short at first; stop before baby is very upset
- Roll a small towel and place under armpits to make position easier
- Place toys or your face within baby's visual range (20–30 cm)
- Tummy time on your lap also works — different surface texture may be better tolerated

### NHS Alignment
- NHS: "at least 30 minutes of tummy time spread throughout the day while awake"
- Can start tummy time on parent's chest from birth — only when parent is wide awake
- Gradually increase time day by day
