# Baby Crying: Understanding and Soothing — KidsHealth (AAP)

## Key Points
- Newborns cry 2–3 hours per day on average during the first 6 weeks — this is completely normal
- Crying peaks during the first 3 months, then gradually decreases
- Most common causes: hunger, tiredness, needing to burp, overstimulation, wet/dirty diaper, temperature discomfort
- Colic definition: crying ≥3 hours/day, ≥3 days/week, for ≥3 weeks — typically starts at 2–5 weeks, resolves by 3–4 months
- Soothing techniques: rocking, walking, singing, pacifier, stroller/car ride, swaddling (under 2 months), warm bath, dimmed lights
- Holding baby against bare chest (skin-to-skin) regulates heart rate, breathing, and body temperature
- NEVER shake a baby — causes shaken baby syndrome and permanent brain damage
- Call doctor immediately if baby has fever ≥100.4°F (38°C) or cannot be consoled for any reason

## Raw Content
Crying is a newborn's primary means of communication. In the first 6 weeks, newborns cry an average of 2–3 hours daily, and this is normal and expected. Crying peaks around 6 weeks of age and then decreases significantly by 3–4 months.

**Common causes of crying:**
- Hunger (most common — feed every 2–3 hours)
- Tiredness and need to sleep
- Gas or need to burp
- Overstimulation (too much noise or activity)
- Wet or soiled diaper
- Too hot or too cold
- Need for comfort and closeness

**Soothing techniques:**
- Rocking or gentle movement (stroller, car ride)
- Singing, humming, or white noise
- Offering a pacifier — sucking is deeply calming for newborns
- Swaddling (effective under 2 months of age)
- Skin-to-skin contact — regulates baby's physiology
- Warm bath and gentle back rub
- Moving to a calm, dimly lit environment

**Colic:**
- Defined as crying ≥3 hours/day, ≥3 days/week, for ≥3 weeks
- Starts around 2–5 weeks; resolves by 3–4 months in most cases
- Cause is unknown; not the parent's fault
- No proven cure; soothing strategies help manage it

**When to call the doctor:**
- Baby has a fever ≥100.4°F (38°C)
- Baby seems in obvious pain (drawing up legs, arching back)
- Nothing soothes the baby at all
- Vomiting, diarrhea, or other concerning symptoms

**Safety reminder:**
- If parent feels overwhelmed, place baby safely in crib and take a 10-minute break
- NEVER shake the baby — shaken baby syndrome causes permanent brain injury or death
