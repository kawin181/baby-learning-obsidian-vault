# Swaddling Techniques

## Summary
Swaddling is the practice of snugly wrapping a newborn in a blanket to mimic the womb environment. When done correctly with hip-healthy technique, it can calm infants and promote sleep. Incorrect swaddling — particularly tight leg wrapping or using a swaddled baby who can roll — carries serious risks including hip dysplasia and SIDS.

## What Research Says

### Benefits
When performed correctly, swaddling can help calm newborns and promote sleep by reducing the startle reflex (Moro reflex) that often wakes babies (AAP HealthyChildren). It replicates the contained feeling of the womb.

### Hip Dysplasia Risk
The most evidence-based safety concern with swaddling is hip dysplasia. Tightly wrapping a baby's legs straight and together prevents the natural hip position (legs bent up and out in a "frog" position), which can lead to developmental dysplasia of the hip (DDH) — abnormal hip joint formation (AAP). Both AAP and NHS strongly emphasize hip-healthy swaddling that allows free leg movement.

### SIDS Risk
Swaddling decreases infant arousal (the ability to wake up from deep sleep), which is a protective mechanism against SIDS. If a swaddled baby rolls onto their stomach and cannot reposition, suffocation risk is high. Swaddling does NOT reduce SIDS risk — it must be used strictly within safe sleep guidelines (AAP).

### Temperature Risk
Wrapping babies in heavy materials or overdressing under a swaddle can cause dangerous overheating — a known SIDS risk factor (NHS). Use only thin, breathable fabrics.

## Practical Guidelines

### How to Swaddle — Step by Step (Hip-Healthy Method)
1. Spread a thin, square muslin or cotton blanket on a flat surface; fold down one corner by about 15 cm
2. Lay baby face-up, head above the folded edge
3. Hold baby's left arm gently against their body; pull left blanket corner across the body and tuck firmly under the right side
4. Fold the bottom of the blanket loosely up toward baby's chin; leave hips room to move (frog position: knees bent up and out)
5. Bring right arm down, pull right corner across and tuck under the left side
6. **Final check**: 2–3 finger widths fit between the swaddle and baby's chest; hips and legs can flex and spread

### Key Safety Rules
| Rule | Why |
|------|-----|
| Back to sleep — always | Rolling prone while swaddled = suffocation risk |
| Thin fabric only (muslin, cot sheet) | Heavy blankets cause overheating (SIDS risk) |
| Head always uncovered | Loose fabric over face = suffocation risk |
| Hips free to bend and spread | Tight legs → developmental hip dysplasia |
| Stop at first sign of rolling | Swaddled rolling = cannot reposition |
| No weighted swaddles | Compromise infant arousal and airway |

### When to Stop
- Stop immediately if baby shows ANY attempt to roll (can occur as early as 2 months)
- Routine stopping age: ~3–4 months
- Transition to arms-free wearable sleep sack (keeps body warm without restricting movement)

### Temperature Check
Check back of baby's neck — it should feel warm, not hot or sweaty. Room temperature: 16–20°C (61–68°F). Remove a layer if neck is sweaty.

## Warning Signs 🚨
- Baby rolls or attempts to roll while swaddled → stop swaddling immediately
- Swaddle repeatedly comes loose during sleep → secure tighter or transition to sleep sack
- Baby consistently sweating or overheating under swaddle → too hot, reduce layers
- Baby's legs appear stiff/extended after swaddling rather than frog-shaped → hip health concern, review technique
- Any fever — do not swaddle during illness
