# Infant Reflux & GERD — AAP (HealthyChildren.org)

## Key Points
- GER (Gastroesophageal Reflux) = movement of stomach contents into esophagus — normal in infants
- GERD = reflux associated with symptoms (pain, poor weight gain, mucosal injury) — pathological
- GER occurs in up to 2/3 of healthy infants
- Onset: ~2–3 weeks of age; peaks 4–5 months; resolves by 9–12 months for most full-term infants
- "Happy spitters" = infants with GER but no pain and adequate weight gain — no treatment needed
- AAP recommends AGAINST acid suppressants (e.g., PPIs) for routine infant reflux — risk of infections and fractures outweigh unclear benefit
- Positioning: upright during and after feeding helps
- Feeding: smaller, more frequent feeds; formula thickening (with physician guidance)

## Raw Content

### Definition
**GER** = normal physiologic process where stomach contents reflux into esophagus, sometimes through mouth and nose. Peaks at 4–5 months; resolves by 9–12 months.

**GERD** = GER that causes complications: poor weight gain, apparent pain (irritability, back-arching, feeding refusal), or mucosal injury visible on endoscopy.

### Prevalence
- GER affects up to 2/3 of healthy infants
- Peaks around 3–4 months of age
- Usually self-resolves; not pathologic in most cases

### Clinical Distinction
- **Happy spitter** = content infant who spits up without distress, gaining weight normally → observation only, no treatment
- **GERD** = irritability, anorexia/feeding refusal, poor weight gain, dysphagia, arching during feeds

### Management
**Conservative (first-line):**
- Upright positioning after feeds
- Smaller, more frequent feedings
- Formula thickening (rice cereal in bottle — only with physician guidance due to aspiration risk)
- Dairy-free trial in breastfed infants if cow's milk protein allergy suspected

**Medications — NOT recommended for routine GER:**
- AAP and Choosing Wisely recommend AGAINST acid suppressants (PPIs, H2 blockers) for infants with simple GER
- Acid suppressants linked to higher risk of serious infections and fractures
- May be considered for confirmed GERD with esophagitis — specialist involvement appropriate

**Surgery:**
- Fundoplication — only for severe, refractory GERD with life-threatening complications

### Warning Signs (see doctor)
- Feeding refusal or back-arching during feeds
- Blood or green color in vomit
- Forceful projectile vomiting (rule out pyloric stenosis)
- Abdominal distension
- Wheezing or coughing (concern for aspiration)
- Poor weight gain or decreased wet diapers
- New onset after 6 months (unusual)
