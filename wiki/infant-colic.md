# Infant Colic

## Summary
Colic is intense, unexplained crying in an otherwise healthy, well-fed baby affecting 10–20% of infants worldwide. It peaks around 6 weeks of age and resolves on its own by 3–4 months. There is no proven cure — parental reassurance, soothing techniques, and safe coping strategies are the mainstay of management.

## What Research Says
- **Definition (Wessel Criteria / Rule of 3):** Crying ≥ 3 hours/day, ≥ 3 days/week, for ≥ 3 weeks in an otherwise healthy infant (Mayo Clinic, AAP)
- **Prevalence:** 10–40% globally (Mayo Clinic); approximately 1 in 5 infants (AAP HealthyChildren); equal across breastfed and formula-fed babies
- **Peak and resolution:** Symptoms peak at 6 weeks; typically resolve by 3–4 months; occasionally persist to 6 months (AAP)
- **Causes are not fully understood.** Proposed mechanisms include: immature digestive system, imbalanced gut microbiome (low Lactobacillus), cow's milk protein sensitivity (<5% of cases), immature or hypersensitive nervous system, parental anxiety feedback loop
- **Medications not effective:** Simethicone (gas drops), gripe water — no scientific evidence. Spinal manipulation/cranial osteopathy — no evidence, potentially harmful
- **Probiotics:** Lactobacillus reuteri shows limited benefit in breastfed infants but evidence remains insufficient for routine recommendation

## Practical Guidelines
**For breastfeeding parents — dietary trial:**
- Eliminate one suspected food at a time: dairy, caffeine, onions, cabbage
- Wait 2 weeks per food before assessing effect
- Reintroduce to confirm if it was the trigger

**For formula-fed infants:**
- Discuss switching to extensively hydrolyzed (protein hydrolysate) formula with pediatrician
- Trial period: 2 weeks

**Soothing techniques (try in sequence):**
1. Rhythmic motion — baby swing, rocking chair, car ride, stroller walk
2. White noise — fan, vacuum sound, steady shushing
3. Pacifier — non-nutritive sucking is soothing
4. Swaddling — snug wrap reduces startle reflex
5. Tummy position — lay baby face-down across parent's forearm or knees; rub back gently (supervised only — never for sleep)
6. Warm bath — brief soak may relax muscle tension
7. Baby carrier — constant rhythmic motion + body warmth
8. Gentle abdominal massage in clockwise circles

**Parental coping (critical):**
- Take turns with a partner; ask family for 1–2 hour breaks
- Put baby down safely in crib and step away briefly if overwhelmed
- Screen for parental burnout and postpartum depression

## Warning Signs 🚨
- Fever ≥ 38°C (100.4°F) — not colic, may be infection
- Vomiting that is forceful or greenish
- Blood in stool
- Baby not gaining weight or feeding poorly
- Crying that sounds different from usual (high-pitched, inconsolable beyond normal colic pattern)
- Bulging fontanelle (soft spot on head)
- Any signs of injury — seek emergency care immediately and **never shake a baby**
