# Baby Nail Care — NHS

## Key Points
- Some babies are born with long nails — trim as soon as needed to prevent self-scratching
- Recommended tools: special baby nail clippers, small round-ended safety scissors, or fine emery board
- Timing: trim when infant is asleep or very calm to minimize movement
- Toenails: same tools apply; trim straight across (not curved)
- Specialized baby tools preferred over adult-sized clippers/scissors

## Raw Content

### Why Nail Care Matters
Some babies are born with long nails that can scratch their face. Nails need to be kept short from birth. NHS recommends trimming promptly when nails are long enough to scratch.

### Tools
- **Special baby nail clippers** — designed for small nails
- **Small round-ended safety scissors** — blunt tip for safety
- **Fine emery board** — gentlest, no cutting required; useful for very young newborns in the first weeks when nails are very soft and thin

### Timing
- Best when baby is asleep or very calm (least movement)
- Some parents find it easiest during a feed when the baby is focused on nursing

### General Context (from NHS washing/bathing guidance)
- Full daily baths are not required — "top and tail" (face, hands, nappy area) is sufficient
- Dry skin thoroughly in skin folds after washing
- Nail care is part of routine daily hygiene alongside cleaning eyes with cooled boiled water

### Ingrown Toenails
Not common in newborns, but toenails should be trimmed straight across (not curved at edges) to prevent ingrown nails as baby grows.
