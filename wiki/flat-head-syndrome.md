# Flat Head Syndrome (Positional Plagiocephaly)

## Summary
Positional plagiocephaly is a flattening of the skull caused by prolonged pressure on a soft newborn head, affecting approximately 20% of infants. It develops mainly in the first 4–12 weeks of life and is almost always preventable with supervised tummy time and position variation. In most cases it resolves naturally by age 1–2 years with no brain impact.

## What Research Says
- **Prevalence**: Estimated 20–50% of infants develop some degree of positional skull deformity (AAP); about 1 in 5 babies at some point (NHS)
- **SIDS campaign link**: Incidence increased after the "Back to Sleep" campaign, since babies now spend more awake time on firm flat surfaces
- **Torticollis co-occurrence**: ~85% of affected babies have torticollis (tight sternocleidomastoid muscle) on one side, causing head-turning preference (AAP)
- **Prognosis**: Purely cosmetic in virtually all cases — no brain compression, no pain, no developmental impact. Most cases resolve by age 1–2 (NHS)
- **Helmet therapy controversy**: AAP supports helmet therapy for moderate-to-severe cases at 5–6 months; NHS does not fund helmets (cost >£1,000, must be worn 23 h/day, evidence insufficient)

## Practical Guidelines

### Prevention (Start from Day 1)
1. **Begin tummy time immediately** — even in hospital after birth, with parent supervision
2. **Build to 1 hour/day** — spread across short sessions (5–10 min each); never leave baby unsupervised prone
3. **Alternate carrying arms** — switch which arm holds baby during feeds and carrying
4. **Rotate crib orientation** — alternate which end of the crib baby's head faces to vary visual stimulus
5. **Limit container time** — minimize time in car seats, bouncers, swings (all immobilize the head in one position)
6. **Use a sling** — front carriers reduce flat-surface head pressure during awake time

### If Flattening Is Noticed
- Start repositioning immediately; outcomes are best before 4 months
- Place toys and stimuli on the less-preferred side to encourage head rotation
- Ask pediatrician to check for torticollis at every visit
- Physical therapy is first-line if torticollis confirmed; teaches neck stretching exercises

### Helmet Therapy
- Considered only for moderate-to-severe deformity unresponsive to repositioning
- Most effective if started before 12 months of age
- AAP supports; NHS does not fund (insufficient evidence)

## Thai Context
No specific Thai guidelines found. General AAP and NHS guidance applies. Note that many Thai families use traditional infant positioning practices — ensure any padded neck supports do not restrict head rotation.

## Warning Signs 🚨
- Baby consistently turns head only one direction (may indicate torticollis)
- Ears visibly misaligned when looking at baby from above
- Forehead asymmetry — one side bulging forward
- Flat area is getting larger or more pronounced after 4 months despite repositioning
- Any concern about head size increasing too rapidly (seek GP — may indicate craniosynostosis, a separate condition requiring surgical evaluation)
