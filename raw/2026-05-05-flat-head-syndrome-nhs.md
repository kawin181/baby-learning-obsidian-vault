# Flat Head Syndrome (Positional Plagiocephaly) — NHS

## Key Points
- Two types: plagiocephaly (flat on one side) and brachycephaly (flat at the back, wide)
- Affects approximately 1 in every 5 babies at some point
- Usually self-corrects by age 1–2 years
- No pain or brain impact — purely cosmetic concern in most cases
- NHS does NOT provide helmets/headbands — insufficient evidence they work
- Back sleeping must NOT change (SIDS prevention) — use awake repositioning instead
- Physical therapy indicated if torticollis (tight neck muscles) present

## Raw Content

### Types
- **Plagiocephaly**: flat on one side; head looks asymmetrical; ears may be misaligned
- **Brachycephaly**: flat at the back; head looks wide; forehead may bulge outward

### Causes
- Extended time sleeping with head pressing against mattress
- Tight neck muscles (torticollis) restricting head rotation
- Premature birth (softer skull at birth)

### Prognosis
- In most cases no concern — no effect on brain, no pain
- Head shape often improves naturally by 1–2 years old
- Early intervention (0–4 months) has best outcomes

### Prevention & Management
**Do:**
- Give supervised tummy time when baby is awake
- Reposition toys and visual stimuli to encourage head turning both ways
- Alternate which side you hold baby during feeding and carrying
- Use a sling or front carrier to reduce time on flat surfaces

**Don't:**
- Change back sleeping position (SIDS risk)
- Allow excessive time in prams, bouncy seats, or car seats

### Treatment
- Positional adjustments usually sufficient
- Physical therapy if torticollis identified
- NHS does not fund helmet therapy — evidence insufficient; helmets cost >£1,000 privately and must be worn 23 hours/day to potentially work

### When to Consult a Doctor
- Any concern about head shape or size
- Baby cannot turn head fully in both directions
- Baby consistently holds head tilted to one side
